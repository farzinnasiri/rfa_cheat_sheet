Measures, Outer Measures, And Lebesgue Measure

  • State and prove the theorem about regularity of the Lebesgue measure.

    Answer. For \(E\subset\mathbb R\), the following are equivalent: \(E\in\mathcal L(\mathbb R)\); for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is an open set \(O\supset E\) with \(\lambda^*(O\setminus E)<\varepsilon\); there is \(G\in G_\delta\) with \(E\subset G\) and \(\lambda^*(G\setminus E)=0\); for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there is a closed set \(C\subset E\) with \(\lambda^*(E\setminus C)<\varepsilon\); there is \(F\in F_\sigma\) with \(F\subset E\) and \(\lambda^*(E\setminus F)=0\). Proof of outer approximation: assume first \(\lambda(E)<\infty\). By the definition of outer measure, choose intervals \(I_k\) covering \(E\) with \(\sum_k\lambda(I_k)<\lambda(E)+\varepsilon\). Put \(O=\bigcup_k I_k\). Then \(O\) is open, \(E\subset O\), and \(\lambda^*(O)<\lambda(E)+\varepsilon\). Since \(E\) is measurable, by excision \(\lambda^*(O\setminus E)=\lambda^*(O)-\lambda(E)<\varepsilon\). For general \(E\), apply this to \(E\cap[-n,n]\) and take a countable union of the open approximations. Taking \(\varepsilon=1/n\) and \(G=\bigcap_n O_n\) gives the \(G_\delta\) form. Inner regularity follows by applying outer regularity to \(E^c\) and taking complements, or first to finite-measure pieces.

  • State and prove the properties of regularity of the Lebesgue measure (it is sufficient to present the proof regarding the outer approximation of measurable sets).

    Answer. Same theorem. In particular, if \(E\in\mathcal L(\mathbb R)\), then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists open \(O\supset E\) such that \(\lambda^*(O\setminus E)<\varepsilon\). If \(\lambda(E)<\infty\), choose open intervals \(I_k\) with \(E\subset\bigcup_k I_k=:O\) and \(\sum_k\lambda(I_k)<\lambda(E)+\varepsilon\). Then \(\lambda^*(O)\le\sum_k\lambda(I_k)<\lambda(E)+\varepsilon\). Since \(E\) is measurable and \(E\subset O\), Caratheodory gives \(\lambda^*(O)=\lambda(E)+\lambda^*(O\setminus E)\), hence \(\lambda^*(O\setminus E)<\varepsilon\). If \(\lambda(E)=\infty\), apply the finite case to \(E_n=E\cap[-n,n]\) with errors \(\varepsilon/2^n\), and take \(O=\bigcup_n O_n\).

  • Let \(\lambda^*\) be the outer measure on R. Starting from it, describe brieﬂy the construction of the Lebesgue \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal L(R)\) and of the Lebesgue measure \(\lambda\) on R. No proofs are required.

    Answer. Define the Lebesgue outer measure by \[ \lambda^*(E)=\inf\left\{\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\ell(I_k): E\subset\bigcup_k I_k,\ I_k\text{ open intervals}\right\}. \] A set \(E\subset\mathbb R\) is Lebesgue measurable if it satisfies Caratheodory’s condition: \[ \lambda^*(A)=\lambda^*(A\cap E)+\lambda^*(A\setminus E)\qquad\forall A\subset\mathbb R. \] The class of all such sets is \(\mathcal L(\mathbb R)\), and Caratheodory’s theorem says that it is a \(\sigma\)-algebra. The Lebesgue measure is the restriction \[ \lambda:=\lambda^*|_{\mathcal L(\mathbb R)}. \] Then \(\lambda\) is a complete measure and extends the length of intervals.

  • What is the Caratheodory condition? Is it essential to formulate it with an equality sign? Justify your answer. Can it exist a set of zero outer measure, which does not fulfill the Caratheodory condition? Justify your answer.

    Answer. Given an outer measure \(\mu^*\) on \(X\), a set \(E\subset X\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable if \[ \mu^*(A)=\mu^*(A\cap E)+\mu^*(A\setminus E)\qquad\forall A\subset X. \] The equality can be replaced by the inequality \[ \mu^*(A)\ge \mu^*(A\cap E)+\mu^*(A\setminus E), \] because the opposite inequality always follows from subadditivity: \[ \mu^*(A)\le \mu^*(A\cap E)+\mu^*(A\setminus E). \] No: every set \(E\) with \(\mu^*(E)=0\) is measurable. Indeed, for any \(A\), \[ \mu^*(A\cap E)\le\mu^*(E)=0, \] and by monotonicity \(A\setminus E\subset A\), so \(\mu^*(A\setminus E)\le\mu^*(A)\). Together with subadditivity this gives equality.

  • Write Caratheodory’s condition and its equivalent version. State Caratheodory’s theorem concerning the generation of a measure as a restriction of an outer measure.

    Answer. Let \(\mu^*\) be an outer measure on \(X\). A set \(E\subset X\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable iff \[ \mu^*(A)=\mu^*(A\cap E)+\mu^*(A\setminus E)\quad\forall A\subset X. \] Equivalently, since subadditivity gives \(\le\), it is enough to require \[ \mu^*(A)\ge\mu^*(A\cap E)+\mu^*(A\setminus E)\quad\forall A\subset X. \] Caratheodory’s theorem: the family \(\mathcal M_{\mu^*}\) of \(\mu^*\)-measurable sets is a \(\sigma\)-algebra, and the restriction \(\mu=\mu^*|_{\mathcal M_{\mu^*}}\) is a complete measure. In particular, if \(\mu^*\) is the Lebesgue outer measure, this gives \((\mathbb R,\mathcal L(\mathbb R),\lambda)\).

  • Given a metric space \((X,d)\), write the deﬁnition of its Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal B(X)\). Provide classes of Borel sets. Characterize \(\mathcal B(R),\mathcal B(R),\mathcal B(RN)\). No proofs are required.

    Answer. The Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra of a metric space \((X,d)\) is \[ \mathcal B(X):=\sigma(\{O\subset X:O\text{ open}\}), \] the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra containing all open subsets of \(X\). Hence open sets, closed sets, countable unions of closed sets (\(F_\sigma\)), countable intersections of open sets (\(G_\delta\)), and all sets obtained from these by countable set operations are Borel. In \(\mathbb R\), \[ \mathcal B(\mathbb R)=\sigma(\text{open intervals})=\sigma(\text{closed intervals})=\sigma((-\infty,a):a\in\mathbb R). \] In \(\mathbb R^N\), \(\mathcal B(\mathbb R^N)\) is generated by open balls, equivalently by open rectangles or products of Borel intervals.

  • Let \((X, M,\mu)\) denote a measure space. Prove the continuity of the measure along monotone sequences, either increasing or decreasing, under suitable further assumptions.

    Answer. If \(E_n\uparrow E\), meaning \(E_n\subset E_{n+1}\) and \(E=\bigcup_nE_n\), then \[ \mu(E_n)\uparrow\mu(E). \] Proof: set \(F_1=E_1\), \(F_n=E_n\setminus E_{n-1}\). Then the \(F_n\) are disjoint, \(E=\bigcup_nF_n\), and \(E_N=\bigcup_{n=1}^N F_n\). Thus \[ \mu(E)=\sum_n\mu(F_n)=\lim_N\sum_{n=1}^N\mu(F_n)=\lim_N\mu(E_N). \] If \(E_n\downarrow E\), meaning \(E_{n+1}\subset E_n\) and \(E=\bigcap_nE_n\), and if \(\mu(E_1)<\infty\), then \[ \mu(E_n)\downarrow\mu(E). \] Proof: \(E_1\setminus E_n\uparrow E_1\setminus E\). By the increasing case, \[ \mu(E_1\setminus E_n)\to\mu(E_1\setminus E). \] Since \(\mu(E_1)<\infty\), subtract from \(\mu(E_1)\) to get \(\mu(E_n)\to\mu(E)\).

  • State and prove the property of continuity of measure along monotone decreasing sequences \(\{E_n\}\) of measurable subsets. Does the property hold, if \(E_1\) (or some of the sets \(E_n\)) has inﬁnite measure? If not, provide a counterexample.

    Answer. If \(E_n\downarrow E\) and \(\mu(E_1)<\infty\), then \(\mu(E_n)\to\mu(E)\). Proof as above: \(E_1\setminus E_n\uparrow E_1\setminus E\), so \[ \mu(E_1\setminus E_n)\to\mu(E_1\setminus E). \] Since \(\mu(E_1)<\infty\), \[ \mu(E_n)=\mu(E_1)-\mu(E_1\setminus E_n)\to\mu(E_1)-\mu(E_1\setminus E)=\mu(E). \] The finite-measure assumption is necessary. In \((\mathbb R,\mathcal L,\lambda)\), let \(E_n=[n,+\infty)\). Then \(E_n\downarrow\varnothing\), but \(\lambda(E_n)=+\infty\) for all \(n\), while \(\lambda(\varnothing)=0\).

Measurable Functions

  • Write the definition of measurable function, for a function defined from a measurable space \((X, A)\) to another measurable space \((X', A')\). State (specifying all the hypotheses) and prove the theorem concerning measurability of the composite function.

    Answer. A map \(f:X\to X'\) is \((\mathcal A,\mathcal A')\)-measurable if \[ f^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal A\qquad\forall E\in\mathcal A'. \] Theorem: if \(f:(X,\mathcal A)\to(X',\mathcal A')\) is measurable and \(g:(X',\mathcal A')\to(X'',\mathcal A'')\) is measurable, then \(g\circ f:(X,\mathcal A)\to(X'',\mathcal A'')\) is measurable. Proof: for \(E\in\mathcal A''\), \(g^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal A'\); hence \[ (g\circ f)^{-1}(E)=f^{-1}(g^{-1}(E))\in\mathcal A. \]

  • Let \((X, M)\) be a measurable space and assume that, for every \(n \in N\), the function \(u_n :X \to R\) is measurable. Prove that the function \(\sup_n u_n\) is Lebesgue measurable too.

    Answer. Let \(u:=\sup_n u_n\). For every \(\alpha\in\mathbb R\), \[ \{u>\alpha\}=\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty \{u_n>\alpha\}. \] Since each \(u_n\) is measurable, each set \(\{u_n>\alpha\}\in\mathcal M\). The countable union belongs to \(\mathcal M\). Hence \(\{u>\alpha\}\in\mathcal M\) for every \(\alpha\), which is an equivalent criterion for measurability. Therefore \(\sup_n u_n\) is measurable.

  • Write the definitions of: a) Borel measurable function \(f : R \to R\); b) Lebesgue measurable function \(f : R \to R\). Prove that continuous functions are both Borel and Lebesgue measurable.

    Answer. A function \(f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) is Borel measurable if \[ f^{-1}(B)\in\mathcal B(\mathbb R)\qquad\forall B\in\mathcal B(\mathbb R). \] It is Lebesgue measurable if \[ f^{-1}(B)\in\mathcal L(\mathbb R)\qquad\forall B\in\mathcal B(\mathbb R). \] If \(f\) is continuous, then for every open set \(O\subset\mathbb R\), \(f^{-1}(O)\) is open, hence Borel. Since open sets generate \(\mathcal B(\mathbb R)\), \(f\) is Borel measurable. Since \(\mathcal B(\mathbb R)\subset\mathcal L(\mathbb R)\), it is also Lebesgue measurable.

  • Let \((X, A)\) be a measurable space. Prove or disprove the following statements:

  1. \(A \in A \iff \chi_A \in M(X, A)\);
  2. \(f \in M(X, A) \iff f^\pm \in M(X, A)\);
  3. \(f \in M(X, A) \iff |f| \in M(X, A)\).

Answer. (1) True. If \(A\in\mathcal A\), then \(\chi_A^{-1}(\{1\})=A\), \(\chi_A^{-1}(\{0\})=A^c\), so \(\chi_A\) is measurable. Conversely, if \(\chi_A\) is measurable, then \(A=\{\chi_A>1/2\}\in\mathcal A\).

  1. True. If \(f\) is measurable, then \(f^+=\max(f,0)\) and \(f^-=\max(-f,0)\) are measurable by composition with continuous functions. Conversely, \[ f=f^+-f^-, \] and algebraic combinations of finite measurable functions are measurable.

  2. Only one implication is true in general: \(f\) measurable implies \(|f|\) measurable, since \(|f|=|\cdot|\circ f\). The converse is false: take a nonmeasurable set \(A\subset X\) and define \(f=\chi_A-\chi_{A^c}\). Then \(|f|=1\) is measurable, but \(f\) is not measurable because \(A=\{f=1\}\) would have to be measurable.

  • Let \((X, A)\) be a measurable space, and let \(f : X \to R\) be a function. State and prove the theorem that relates the measurability of \(f\) to the measurability of the sets \(\{x \in X : f(x) > \alpha\}\), \(\{x \in X : f(x) < \alpha\}\), \(\{x \in X : f(x) \ge \alpha\}\), \(\{x \in X : f(x) \le \alpha\}\) for every \(\alpha \in R\).

    Answer. For \(f:X\to\overline{\mathbb R}\), the following are equivalent: \(f\) is measurable; \(\{f>\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\); \(\{f\ge\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\); \(\{f<\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\); \(\{f\le\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\). Proof: if \(f\) is measurable, these sets are preimages of Borel rays. Conversely, the families of rays \((\alpha,+\infty)\), \([\alpha,+\infty)\), \((-\infty,\alpha)\), \((-\infty,\alpha]\) generate the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra, so measurability of all their preimages implies measurability of \(f\). The conditions are mutually equivalent using complements and countable limits, e.g. \[ \{f\ge\alpha\}=\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}\{f>\alpha-1/n\},\qquad \{f<\alpha\}=\{f\ge\alpha\}^c. \]

Lebesgue Integral And Convergence Theorems

  • Write the definition of the the Lebesgue integral for functions defined on a measure space \((X, M, \mu)\), in the case of:
  1. nonnegative, simple measurable functions;
  2. nonnegative measurable functions;
  3. integrable functions (to be defined). No proofs are required.

Answer. If \(s\in S^+(X,\mathcal M)\) has canonical form \[ s=\sum_{k=1}^m c_k\chi_{E_k},\qquad c_k\ge0,\ E_k\in\mathcal M, \] with the \(E_k\) disjoint, define \[ \int_X s\,d\mu:=\sum_{k=1}^m c_k\mu(E_k). \] If \(f\in\mathcal M^+(X,\mathcal M)\), define \[ \int_X f\,d\mu:=\sup\left\{\int_Xs\,d\mu:\ s\in S^+,\ 0\le s\le f\right\}. \] Equivalently, if \(s_n\uparrow f\), then \(\int f=\lim_n\int s_n\). For a measurable \(f\), define \(f^+=\max(f,0)\), \(f^-=\max(-f,0)\). The function \(f\) is integrable iff \[ \int_X f^+\,d\mu<\infty,\qquad \int_X f^-\,d\mu<\infty, \] equivalently \(\int_X|f|\,d\mu<\infty\). Then \[ \int_X f\,d\mu:=\int_X f^+\,d\mu-\int_X f^-\,d\mu. \]

  • State and prove the vanishing lemma for nonnegative measurable functions.

    Answer. If \(f\in\mathcal M^+(X,\mathcal A)\) and \[ \int_X f\,d\mu=0, \] then \(f=0\) a.e. Proof: it is enough to prove \(\mu(\{f>0\})=0\). Since \[ \{f>0\}=\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}\{f>1/n\}, \] it suffices to prove each set has measure zero. By Chebyshev’s inequality, \[ \mu(\{f>1/n\})\le n\int_X f\,d\mu=0. \] Hence \(\mu(\{f>0\})=0\), so \(f=0\) a.e.

  • State and prove the monotone convergence theorem.

    Answer. Let \(f_n\in\mathcal M^+(X,\mathcal A)\), \(f_n\le f_{n+1}\), and \(f_n\to f\) pointwise. Then \(f\in\mathcal M^+\) and \[ \int_X f\,d\mu=\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_X f_n\,d\mu. \] Proof: since \(f_n\le f\), \(\lim_n\int f_n\le\int f\). For the reverse inequality, take a simple \(s\) with \(0\le s\le f\) and fix \(\varepsilon\in(0,1)\). Define \[ E_n=\{f_n\ge(1-\varepsilon)s\}. \] Then \(E_n\uparrow X\), and \[ \int f_n\ge\int_{E_n}f_n\ge(1-\varepsilon)\int_{E_n}s. \] Passing to the limit and using continuity from below gives \[ \lim_n\int f_n\ge(1-\varepsilon)\int s. \] Let \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\), then take the supremum over simple \(s\le f\), obtaining \(\lim_n\int f_n\ge\int f\).

  • State and prove Fatou’s Lemma.

    Answer. If \(f_n\in\mathcal M^+(X,\mathcal A)\), then \[ \int_X\liminf_{n\to\infty}f_n\,d\mu\le\liminf_{n\to\infty}\int_X f_n\,d\mu. \] Proof: define \[ g_k:=\inf_{m\ge k}f_m. \] Then \(g_k\in\mathcal M^+\), \(g_k\uparrow\liminf_n f_n\), and \(g_k\le f_m\) for all \(m\ge k\). Thus \[ \int g_k\,d\mu\le\inf_{m\ge k}\int f_m\,d\mu. \] Letting \(k\to\infty\), the left-hand side converges by the monotone convergence theorem, giving the result.

  • State and prove the Lebesgue (dominated convergence) theorem.

    Answer. Let \(f_n\) be measurable, \(f_n\to f\) a.e., and suppose there exists \(g\in L^1(X)\) with \[ |f_n|\le g\quad\text{a.e. for all }n. \] Then \(f\in L^1\), \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^1\), and \[ \int_X f_n\,d\mu\to\int_X f\,d\mu. \] Proof: by a.e. convergence, \(|f|\le g\), so \(f\in L^1\). Also \(|f_n-f|\le2g\). Set \(h_n=2g-|f_n-f|\ge0\). By Fatou, \[ \int 2g\,d\mu=\int\liminf h_n\,d\mu\le\liminf\int h_n\,d\mu =2\int g\,d\mu-\limsup\int|f_n-f|\,d\mu. \] Hence \(\limsup\int|f_n-f|\le0\), so \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^1\). Finally, \[ \left|\int f_n-\int f\right|\le\int|f_n-f|\to0. \]

  • State and prove the (Lebesgue) dominated convergence theorem. State a sufficient condition in order to apply the theorem, when the sequence of functions is defined on a set with finite measure.

    Answer. DCT is as above. A useful sufficient condition on a finite measure space \(\mu(X)<\infty\) is: if \(f_n\to f\) a.e. and \(|f_n|\le M\) a.e. for a constant \(M\), then \(g\equiv M\in L^1(X)\) is an integrable dominating function. Hence \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^1\) and \(\int f_n\to\int f\).

  • State and prove the theorem of absolute continuity of the Lebesgue integral.

    Answer. If \(f\in L^1(X,\mathcal A,\mu)\), then for every \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists \(\delta>0\) such that, for every \(E\in\mathcal A\), \[ \mu(E)<\delta\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \int_E |f|\,d\mu<\varepsilon. \] Proof: since \(f\in L^1\), choose \(M>0\) such that \[ \int_{\{|f|>M\}}|f|\,d\mu<\varepsilon/2. \] For any measurable \(E\), \[ \int_E|f|\,d\mu=\int_{E\cap\{|f|\le M\}}|f|+\int_{E\cap\{|f|>M\}}|f| \le M\mu(E)+\varepsilon/2. \] Choose \(\delta=\varepsilon/(2M)\). If \(\mu(E)<\delta\), then \(\int_E|f|<\varepsilon\).

  • State and prove the Hölder inequality.

    Answer. Let \(1<p,q<\infty\), \(1/p+1/q=1\). If \(f\in L^p\), \(g\in L^q\), then \(fg\in L^1\) and \[ \int_X |fg|\,d\mu\le\|f\|_p\|g\|_q. \] If one norm is zero the result is trivial. Otherwise set \(F=|f|/\|f\|_p\), \(G=|g|/\|g\|_q\). Young’s inequality gives \[ FG\le \frac{F^p}{p}+\frac{G^q}{q}. \] Integrating, \[ \int FG\le \frac1p\int F^p+\frac1q\int G^q=\frac1p+\frac1q=1. \] Multiplying by \(\|f\|_p\|g\|_q\) gives Hölder.

Convergence Of Functions And L1

  • Let \((X, A, \mu)\) be a complete measure space, and let \(\{f_n\}_{n\in N} \subset L^1(X)\), \(f \in L^1(X)\). Give the following definitions:
  1. \(f_n \to f\) in \(L^1(X)\);
  2. \(f_n \to f\) in measure in \(X\). Discuss the validity of the following implications: (i) (a) \(\Rightarrow\) (b); (ii) (b) \(\Rightarrow\) (a). If yes, give a proof; if not, provide a counterexample.

Answer. \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^1\) iff \[ \|f_n-f\|_1=\int_X|f_n-f|\,d\mu\to0. \] \(f_n\to f\) in measure iff for every \(\varepsilon>0\), \[ \mu(\{|f_n-f|\ge\varepsilon\})\to0. \] \(L^1\Rightarrow\) measure: by Chebyshev, \[ \mu(\{|f_n-f|\ge\varepsilon\})\le\frac1\varepsilon\int_X|f_n-f|\,d\mu\to0. \] The converse is false. On \((0,1)\), let \(f_n=n\chi_{(0,1/n)}\). Then \(f_n\to0\) in measure because \(\lambda(\{|f_n|>\varepsilon\})=1/n\) for \(n>\varepsilon\), but \[ \|f_n\|_1=n\lambda(0,1/n)=1, \] so \(f_n\not\to0\) in \(L^1\).

  • Consider \(f_n,n \in N\) and \(f\), measurable functions from a measure space \((X, M,\mu)\) to \(R\). Write the following deﬁnitions:
  1. \(\{f_n\}_n\) converges to \(f\) a.e. in \(X\);
  2. \(\{f_n\}_n\) converges to \(f\) in the \(L^1\)-sense. What are the relations between 1. and 2.?

Answer. \(f_n\to f\) a.e. in \(X\) iff there exists \(N\in\mathcal M\), \(\mu(N)=0\), such that \(f_n(x)\to f(x)\) for every \(x\in X\setminus N\). \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^1\) iff \(\int_X|f_n-f|\,d\mu\to0\). In general neither full implication holds. \(L^1\) convergence implies convergence in measure and therefore admits a subsequence converging a.e.; it does not imply a.e. convergence of the full sequence. A counterexample is the typewriter sequence on \([0,1]\), which converges to \(0\) in \(L^1\) but not pointwise anywhere. A.e. convergence does not imply \(L^1\) convergence: \(f_n=n\chi_{(0,1/n)}\) on \((0,1)\) converges a.e. to \(0\), but \(\|f_n\|_1=1\). With domination \(|f_n|\le g\in L^1\), a.e. convergence implies \(L^1\) convergence by DCT.

  • Consider measurable functions \(f_n : I \to R\), where \(I \subseteq R\) is a Lebesgue measurable set. For each of the following cases, provide an explicit example or explain why no such example exists:
  1. Suppose \(\lambda(I) < \infty\). Does there exist a sequence \(\{f_n\}\) such that \(f_n \to f\) almost everywhere in \(I\), but \(f_n\) does not converge to \(f\) in measure?
  2. Suppose \(\lambda(I) = \infty\). Does there exist a sequence \(\{f_n\}\) such that \(f_n \to f\) almost everywhere on \(I\), but \(f_n\) does not converge to \(f\) in measure?
  3. Does there exist a sequence \(\{f_n\}\) such that \(f_n \to f\) in measure, but \(f_n\) does not converge to \(f\) in \(L^1\)?

Answer. (1) No. If \(\lambda(I)<\infty\), a.e. convergence implies convergence in measure. This is Egorov-type measure control: for fixed \(\varepsilon>0\), the sets where \(|f_n-f|\ge\varepsilon\) decrease to a null set in measure.

  1. Yes. On \(I=\mathbb R\), take \(f_n=\chi_{[n,+\infty)}\). Then \(f_n(x)\to0\) for every fixed \(x\), but for \(\varepsilon=1/2\), \[ \lambda(\{|f_n|>\varepsilon\})=\lambda([n,+\infty))=+\infty, \] so there is no convergence in measure.

  2. Yes. On \((0,1)\), \(f_n=n\chi_{(0,1/n)}\). Then \(f_n\to0\) in measure, but \(\|f_n\|_1=1\), so there is no \(L^1\) convergence.

  • Let \((X,A,\mu)\) be a (complete) measure space.
  1. Deﬁne the convergence in mean and the convergence in measure.
  2. Prove that the convergence in mean implies the convergence in measure. Is the converse true? Justify the answer.

Answer. Convergence in mean means \(L^1\) convergence: \[ \int_X|f_n-f|\,d\mu\to0. \] Convergence in measure means \[ \forall\varepsilon>0,\quad \mu(\{|f_n-f|\ge\varepsilon\})\to0. \] If \(f_n\to f\) in mean, then by Chebyshev \[ \mu(\{|f_n-f|\ge\varepsilon\})\le\frac1\varepsilon\int_X|f_n-f|\,d\mu\to0. \] The converse is false: \(f_n=n\chi_{(0,1/n)}\) on \((0,1)\) converges to \(0\) in measure, but \(\|f_n\|_1=1\).

  • Consider \(f_n, n \in N\) and \(f\), measurable functions from a measure set \((X, M,\mu)\) to \(R\). Write the following deﬁnitions:
  1. \(\{f_n\}_n\) converges to \(f\) in measure;
  2. \(\{f_n\}_n\) converges to \(f\) in the \(L^1\)-sense. Which is the relation between 1. and 2.? No proofs are required.

Answer. \(f_n\to f\) in measure iff for every \(\varepsilon>0\), \[ \mu(\{|f_n-f|\ge\varepsilon\})\to0. \] \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^1\) iff \[ \|f_n-f\|_1=\int_X|f_n-f|\,d\mu\to0. \] \(L^1\) convergence implies convergence in measure. The converse is false in general; for example \(f_n=n\chi_{(0,1/n)}\) on \((0,1)\) converges in measure to \(0\), but not in \(L^1\).

Absolutely Continuous And Bounded Variation Functions

  • Write the following deﬁnitions:
  1. absolutely continuous function in \([a,b]\);
  2. weak derivative of a function deﬁned in \([a,b]\);
  3. \(W^{1,1}([a,b])\). What is the relation between \(W^{1,1}([a,b])\) and the set of absolutely continuous functions in \([a,b]\)? No proofs are required.

Answer. Lebesgue Points And Fundamental Theorem Of Calculus

  • Let \(f : [a, b] \to R\).
  1. Write the definition of Lebesgue point.
  2. What can be said about the Lebesgue measure of the set \(A = \{x \in [a, b] : x\) is not a Lebesgue point of \(f\}\)?
  3. State the first fundamental theorem of calculus for functions in \(L^1((a, b))\). No proofs are required.

Answer. For \(f\in L^1(a,b)\), \(x_0\in(a,b)\) is a Lebesgue point of \(f\) if \[ \lim_{h\to0}\frac1{|h|}\int_{x_0}^{x_0+h}|f(t)-f(x_0)|\,dt=0. \] By the Lebesgue differentiation theorem, almost every point is a Lebesgue point: \[ \lambda(A)=0. \] First fundamental theorem for \(L^1\): if \(f\in L^1(a,b)\) and \[ F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt, \] then \(F\) is differentiable a.e. and \[ F'(x)=f(x)\qquad\text{for a.e. }x\in(a,b). \]

  • Let \(f\in L^1([a,b])\).
  1. Give the deﬁnition of Lebesgue point for \(f\) and show an example of non-Lebesgue point.
  2. State and prove the ﬁrst fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Answer. \(x_0\) is a Lebesgue point if \[ \lim_{h\to0}\frac1{|h|}\int_{x_0}^{x_0+h}|f(t)-f(x_0)|\,dt=0. \] Example: for \(f=\chi_{(0,1)}\), the point \(0\) is not a Lebesgue point if we define \(f(0)=0\), since averages near \(0\) do not approach \(0\).

First FTC: if \(f\in L^1(a,b)\) and \(F(x)=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt\), then \(F'(x)=f(x)\) a.e. Proof: let \(x\) be a Lebesgue point of \(f\). Then \[ \frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h}-f(x)=\frac1h\int_x^{x+h}(f(t)-f(x))\,dt. \] Taking absolute values, \[ \left|\frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h}-f(x)\right| \le \frac1{|h|}\int_x^{x+h}|f(t)-f(x)|\,dt\to0. \] Hence \(F'(x)=f(x)\) at every Lebesgue point, and almost every point is a Lebesgue point.

Banach Spaces, Metric Spaces, Compactness, And Separability

  • Write the definition of separable metric space. Show that \(C^0([a, b])\) is separable.

    Answer. A metric space \((X,d)\) is separable if it has a countable dense subset. In \(C^0([a,b])\) with the sup norm, polynomials with rational coefficients are countable. By Weierstrass theorem, polynomials are dense in \(C^0([a,b])\). Given a real-coefficient polynomial \(p\), approximate its coefficients by rationals to get a rational-coefficient polynomial \(q\) close to \(p\) uniformly on \([a,b]\). Therefore rational polynomials are countable and dense, so \(C^0([a,b])\) is separable.

  • Let \((X,d)\) be a metric space.

  1. Deﬁne completeness and separability for \(X\).
  2. Give an example of separable inﬁnite-dimensional normed vector space. Prove that \(L^\infty([a,b])\) is not separable.

Answer. \(X\) is complete if every Cauchy sequence converges in \(X\). It is separable if it contains a countable dense subset. An example of a separable infinite-dimensional normed space is \(C^0([a,b])\) with \(\|\cdot\|_\infty\), or \(\ell^p\) for \(1\le p<\infty\).

\(L^\infty([a,b])\) is not separable. For \(t\in[a,b]\), define \(f_t=\chi_{[a,t]}\). If \(s<t\), then \[ \|f_t-f_s\|_\infty=1. \] Thus there is an uncountable family whose mutual distances are \(1\). If \(L^\infty\) were separable, a countable dense set would have to contain points within radius \(1/3\) of each \(f_t\), but the balls \(B(f_t,1/3)\) are pairwise disjoint. A countable dense set cannot meet uncountably many disjoint balls in this way. Contradiction.

  • Let \((X, \|\cdot\|)\) be a Banach space. Is it true that \(C \subset X\) is compact \(\Longleftrightarrow C\) is closed and bounded? Is it true under some assumption on \(X\)? State and prove the Riesz theorem (about the compactness of the unit ball; if you use a lemma, state it, but it is not necessary to write its proof).

    Answer. In every metric space, compact implies closed and bounded. The converse is false in infinite-dimensional Banach spaces. It is true in finite-dimensional normed spaces. Riesz theorem: a normed space \(X\) is finite-dimensional iff its closed unit ball \(\overline B_1(0)\) is compact. Proof of the noncompact direction: assume \(X\) is infinite-dimensional. Choose \(x_1\) with \(\|x_1\|=1\). Having chosen \(x_1,\dots,x_n\), set \(Y_n=\operatorname{span}\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}\), a closed proper subspace. By Riesz lemma, there exists \(x_{n+1}\) with \(\|x_{n+1}\|=1\) and \(\operatorname{dist}(x_{n+1},Y_n)\ge1/2\). Then \(\|x_n-x_m\|\ge1/2\) for \(m<n\), so \(\{x_n\}\subset\overline B_1(0)\) has no Cauchy subsequence, hence no convergent subsequence. Therefore the unit ball is not compact.

Hahn-Banach, Duality, Bidual, And Reflexivity

  • State the Hahn-Banach theorem. Deﬁne the canonical map \(\tau\) between \(X\) and its dual \(X^*\), prove that \(\tau\) is an isometry, and give the deﬁnition of reﬂexivity.

    Answer. Hahn-Banach extension theorem: if \(Y\subset X\) is a subspace of a normed space and \(f\in Y^*\), then there exists \(F\in X^*\) such that \(F|_Y=f\) and \(\|F\|_{X^*}=\|f\|_{Y^*}\). The canonical map is actually from \(X\) to the bidual \(X^{**}\): \[ \tau:X\to X^{**},\qquad \tau(x)(L)=L(x)\quad\forall L\in X^*. \] It is linear. Moreover, \[ \|\tau(x)\|=\sup_{\|L\|\le1}|L(x)|\le\|x\|. \] If \(x\ne0\), Hahn-Banach gives \(L_x\in X^*\) with \(\|L_x\|=1\) and \(L_x(x)=\|x\|\). Thus \(\|\tau(x)\|\ge\|x\|\), so \(\|\tau(x)\|=\|x\|\). \(X\) is reflexive if \(\tau\) is onto \(X^{**}\).

  • State the Hahn-Banach theorem and its consequences (3 corollaries). No proofs are required.

    Answer. Hahn-Banach: every continuous linear functional on a subspace of a normed space extends to the whole space with the same norm. Three standard corollaries: (1) if \(x_0\ne0\), there exists \(L\in X^*\) with \(\|L\|=1\) and \(L(x_0)=\|x_0\|\); (2) if \(L(x)=L(y)\) for every \(L\in X^*\), then \(x=y\), i.e. the dual separates points; (3) if \(Y\subset X\) is a subspace and \(x_0\notin\overline Y\), then there exists \(L\in X^*\) such that \(L|_Y=0\) and \(L(x_0)\ne0\).

  • Let \(X\) be a Banach space, and let \(X^{**}\) denote its bidual.

  1. Provide the deﬁnition of the canonical (evaluation) map \(\tau :X \to X^{**}\).
  2. List the principal properties of \(\tau\).
  3. What does it mean that \(X\) is reﬂexive?

Answer. The bidual is \(X^{**}=(X^*)^*\). For \(x\in X\), define \(\tau(x)\in X^{**}\) by \[ \tau(x)(L)=L(x)\qquad\forall L\in X^*. \] The map \(\tau:X\to X^{**}\) is linear and isometric: \[ \|\tau(x)\|_{X^{**}}=\|x\|_X. \] Hence it is injective and identifies \(X\) with a closed subspace of \(X^{**}\). The proof of isometry uses Hahn-Banach as above. \(X\) is reflexive if \(\tau(X)=X^{**}\), i.e. every element of the bidual is evaluation at some \(x\in X\).

Weak And Weak-Star Convergence

  • Let \(X\) a Banach space, let \(\{T_n\}_{n\in N}\subset X^*\), \(T\in X^*\). Write the following deﬁnitions:
  1. \(T_n\to T\) as \(n\to+\infty\) weakly;
  2. \(T_n\to T\) as \(n\to+\infty\) weakly*. Discuss the relations between (i) and (ii), providing all details and adding some hypothesis, if needed.

Answer. In \(X^*\), weak convergence means \[ T_n\rightharpoonup T \iff \Phi(T_n)\to\Phi(T)\qquad\forall\Phi\in X^{**}. \] Weak* convergence means \[ T_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}T \iff T_n(x)\to T(x)\qquad\forall x\in X. \] Weak convergence implies weak* convergence: for each \(x\in X\), \(\tau(x)\in X^{**}\), and \(\tau(x)(T_n)=T_n(x)\). Thus \(\Phi(T_n)\to\Phi(T)\) for all \(\Phi\in X^{**}\) implies \(T_n(x)\to T(x)\). The converse is false in general. If \(X\) is reflexive, then \(\tau(X)=X^{**}\), so weak and weak* convergence in \(X^*\) coincide.

  • Let \(X\) be a Banach space, and let \(X^*\) denote its dual. Let \(\{L_n\}\subset X^*,L\in X^*\). Write the following deﬁnitions:
  1. \(\{L_n\}\) converges to \(L\) in the weak sense as \(n\to+\infty\);
  2. \(\{L_n\}\) converges to \(L\) in the weak* sense as \(n\to+\infty\). Which is the relation between 1. and 2.? No proofs are required.

Answer. Same definitions as above: \[ L_n\rightharpoonup L\text{ in }X^* \iff \Phi(L_n)\to\Phi(L)\quad\forall\Phi\in X^{**}, \] while \[ L_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}L \iff L_n(x)\to L(x)\quad\forall x\in X. \] Weak convergence implies weak* convergence. If \(X\) is reflexive, the two notions coincide. Without reflexivity, weak* convergence is generally weaker.

  • Write the definitions of weak convergence and of weak* convergence. Which is the relation between weak and weak* convergence? Justify your answer.

    Answer. In a normed space \(X\), \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\) weakly if \[ L(x_n)\to L(x)\qquad\forall L\in X^*. \] In a dual space \(X^*\), \(L_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}L\) weak* if \[ L_n(x)\to L(x)\qquad\forall x\in X. \] For sequences in \(X^*\), \[ L_n\rightharpoonup L \Rightarrow L_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}L, \] because evaluation at each \(x\) is the bidual element \(\tau(x)\in X^{**}\). The converse holds if \(X\) is reflexive and fails in general.

  • Let \(X\) be a Banach space.

  1. Define strong convergence and weak convergence for a sequence in \(X\).
  2. Does strong convergence imply weak convergence?
  3. Does weak convergence imply strong convergence? For both questions, provide either a proof or a counterexample.

Answer. Strong convergence means \(\|x_n-x\|_X\to0\). Weak convergence means \[ L(x_n)\to L(x)\qquad\forall L\in X^*. \] Strong implies weak: for \(L\in X^*\), \[ |L(x_n)-L(x)|\le\|L\|\,\|x_n-x\|\to0. \] Weak does not imply strong in general. In \(\ell^2\), let \(e_n\) be the canonical basis. For every \(y\in\ell^2\), \[ \langle e_n,y\rangle=y_n\to0, \] so \(e_n\rightharpoonup0\). But \(\|e_n\|=1\) for all \(n\), so \(e_n\not\to0\) strongly.

  • Let \(X\) be a Banach space and let \(\{x_n\}\) be a sequence that converges weakly to \(x\) in \(X\).
  1. Is it true that the sequence \(\{x_n\}\) is necessarily bounded in \(X\)?
  2. What can be said about \(\liminf \|x_n\|\)?
  3. If \(T \in L(X)\), what can be said about the sequence \(\{T(x_n)\}\)?
  4. If \(\{L_n\} \subset X^*\) converges strongly to \(L\), what can be said about the sequence \(\{L_n(x_n)\}\)? No proofs are required.

Answer. Yes, weakly convergent sequences are bounded: \[ \sup_n\|x_n\|<\infty. \] The norm is weakly lower semicontinuous: \[ \|x\|\le\liminf_{n\to\infty}\|x_n\|. \] If \(T\in\mathcal L(X)\), then \[ Tx_n\rightharpoonup Tx, \] because \(L\circ T\in X^*\) for every \(L\in X^*\). Banach-Steinhaus And Baire

  • State and prove the Banach-Steinhaus theorem (or uniformly boundedness principle).

    Answer. Let \(X\) be Banach, \(Y\) normed, and \(\mathcal F\subset\mathcal L(X,Y)\). If \(\mathcal F\) is pointwise bounded, i.e. \[ \forall x\in X,\quad \sup_{T\in\mathcal F}\|Tx\|<\infty, \] then \(\mathcal F\) is uniformly bounded: \[ \sup_{T\in\mathcal F}\|T\|<\infty. \] Proof: define \[ C_n=\{x\in X:\|Tx\|\le n\ \forall T\in\mathcal F\}. \] Each \(C_n\) is closed, and pointwise boundedness gives \(X=\bigcup_n C_n\). By Baire theorem, some \(C_{n_0}\) has nonempty interior, so \(B_\varepsilon(x_0)\subset C_{n_0}\). If \(\|z\|\le\varepsilon\), then \(x_0,z+x_0\in C_{n_0}\), hence \[ \|Tz\|\le\|T(x_0+z)\|+\|Tx_0\|\le2n_0. \] For \(x\ne0\), apply this to \(z=\varepsilon x/\|x\|\): \[ \|Tx\|\le\frac{2n_0}{\varepsilon}\|x\|. \] Hence \(\|T\|\le2n_0/\varepsilon\) for all \(T\in\mathcal F\).

  • Write the statements of the following results, providing the relevant definitions:

  1. the Banach-Steinhaus theorem;
  2. the Baire lemma. No proofs are required.

Answer. Pointwise bounded means \(\sup_{T\in\mathcal F}\|Tx\|<\infty\) for each fixed \(x\in X\). Uniformly bounded means \(\sup_{T\in\mathcal F}\|T\|<\infty\). Banach-Steinhaus: if \(X\) is Banach, \(Y\) is normed, and \(\mathcal F\subset\mathcal L(X,Y)\) is pointwise bounded, then \(\mathcal F\) is uniformly bounded.

Baire lemma/theorem: if \(X\) is a complete metric space and \[ X=\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty}C_n \] with each \(C_n\) closed, then at least one \(C_n\) has nonempty interior. Equivalently, a complete metric space cannot be a countable union of closed sets with empty interior.

  • Let \(X, Y\) be Banach spaces, let \(F \subset L (X, Y)\). Write the following two definitions:
  1. \(F\) is point-wise bounded; (ii) \(F\) is uniformly bounded. State the Banach-Steinhaus theorem. No proofs are required.

Answer. \(F\) is point-wise bounded if \[ \forall x\in X,\quad \sup_{T\in F}\|Tx\|_Y<\infty. \] It is uniformly bounded if \[ \sup_{T\in F}\|T\|_{\mathcal L(X,Y)}<\infty. \] Banach-Steinhaus theorem: if \(X\) is Banach and \(F\subset\mathcal L(X,Y)\) is point-wise bounded, then \(F\) is uniformly bounded.

Banach-Alaoglu

  • State the Banach-Alaoglu theorem. State and prove the variant of the Banach-Alaoglu theorem in reflexive spaces.

    Answer. Banach-Alaoglu: for any normed space \(X\), the closed unit ball of \(X^*\) is compact in the weak* topology \(\sigma(X^*,X)\). Sequential variant: if \(X\) is separable, every bounded sequence in \(X^*\) has a weak* convergent subsequence.

    Reflexive variant: if \(X\) is a separable reflexive Banach space, then every bounded sequence in \(X\) admits a weakly convergent subsequence. Proof: let \(\{x_n\}\) be bounded. Through the canonical isometry \(\tau:X\to X^{**}\), \(\{\tau(x_n)\}\) is bounded in \(X^{**}=(X^*)^*\). Since \(X\) is separable and reflexive, \(X^*\) is separable. By Banach-Alaoglu in the dual of \(X^*\), there is a subsequence \(\tau(x_{n_k})\) weak* converging to some \(\Phi\in X^{**}\). Reflexivity gives \(\Phi=\tau(x)\) for some \(x\in X\). Thus for all \(L\in X^*\), \[ L(x_{n_k})=\tau(x_{n_k})(L)\to\tau(x)(L)=L(x), \] so \(x_{n_k}\rightharpoonup x\).

  • Write the statement of the Banach-Alaoglu theorem (both versions). Provide an example of its application. No proofs are required.

    Answer. Standard Banach-Alaoglu: the closed unit ball of \(X^*\) is weak* compact. Sequential version: if \(X\) is separable, every bounded sequence in \(X^*\) has a weak* convergent subsequence. Reflexive version: if \(X\) is reflexive and separable, every bounded sequence in \(X\) has a weakly convergent subsequence. Application: since \(L^\infty(\Omega)=(L^1(\Omega))^*\) in the usual identification and \(L^1\) is separable in common finite-measure settings, every bounded sequence in \(L^\infty\) admits a weak* convergent subsequence: \[ f_{n_k}\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}f,\qquad \int f_{n_k}g\to\int fg\quad\forall g\in L^1. \]

Closed Operators And Closed Graph Theorem

  • Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be Banach spaces, let \(T :X\to Y\) be a linear operator.
  1. Give the deﬁnition of closed operator \(T :X\to Y\).
  2. Given a linear operator \(T : X \to Y\), explain the relationship between being closed and having a closed graph.
  3. State the Closed Graph Theorem.

Answer. \(T\) is closed if whenever \(x_n\to x\) in \(X\) and \(Tx_n\to y\) in \(Y\), then \(y=Tx\). The graph is \[ G(T)=\{(x,Tx):x\in X\}\subset X\times Y. \] For a linear operator, \(T\) is closed iff \(G(T)\) is closed in \(X\times Y\). Indeed, convergence \((x_n,Tx_n)\to(x,y)\) is exactly \(x_n\to x\) and \(Tx_n\to y\). Closed graph theorem: if \(X,Y\) are Banach spaces and \(T:X\to Y\) is linear and closed, then \(T\) is bounded, i.e. \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\).

  • Let \(X, Y\) be Banach spaces. Let \(T : X \to Y\) be a linear operator. Consider the following properties: (a) the graph of \(T\) is closed; (b) the operator \(T\) is closed.
  1. Write the definitions of (a) and (b).
  2. What is the relation between (a) and (b)?
  3. State the closed graph theorem. No proofs are required for (i), (ii), (iii).

Answer. \(G(T)=\{(x,Tx):x\in X\}\). The graph is closed if it is a closed subset of \(X\times Y\). The operator is closed if \(x_n\to x\) and \(Tx_n\to y\) imply \(y=Tx\). These two properties are equivalent. Closed graph theorem: a closed linear operator between Banach spaces is continuous/bounded.

  • Write the definitions of: closed operator; graph of an operator. Show that an operator is linear and closed if and only if its graph is closed. State and prove the closed graph theorem.

    Answer. Definitions and equivalence as above. Proof of closed graph theorem: define \[ \|x\|_G:=\|x\|_X+\|Tx\|_Y. \] Since \(T\) is closed, \(G(T)\) is a closed subspace of the Banach space \(X\times Y\), hence \(G(T)\) is Banach. The map \(x\mapsto(x,Tx)\) is an isometry from \((X,\|\cdot\|_G)\) onto \(G(T)\), so \((X,\|\cdot\|_G)\) is Banach. The identity \[ I:(X,\|\cdot\|_G)\to(X,\|\cdot\|_X) \] is linear, bijective, and bounded. By the inverse bounded mapping theorem, \(I^{-1}\) is bounded, so \(\|x\|_G\le C\|x\|_X\). Hence \[ \|Tx\|_Y\le \|x\|_G\le C\|x\|_X, \] and \(T\) is bounded.

Compact Operators And Finite Rank Operators

  • Write the deﬁnition of compact operator. State and prove the theorem relating compactness and surjectivity of an operator. After deﬁning what is meant by a weak-to-strong continuous operator, describe the relationship between such operators and compact operators.

    Answer. \(K:X\to Y\) is compact if it maps bounded sets into relatively compact sets; equivalently, for every bounded sequence \(x_n\), \(Kx_n\) has a strongly convergent subsequence. A compact operator \(K:X\to Y\) with \(Y\) infinite-dimensional cannot be surjective. If it were surjective, the open mapping theorem would give \(\delta>0\) such that \(B_Y(0,\delta)\subset K(B_X(0,1))\). But compactness gives \(\overline{K(B_X(0,1))}\) compact, hence \(\overline{B_Y(0,\delta)}\) compact, contradicting Riesz theorem in infinite-dimensional \(Y\).

    \(T\) is weak-to-strong continuous if \[ x_n\rightharpoonup x \text{ in }X \quad\Rightarrow\quad Tx_n\to Tx \text{ strongly in }Y. \] If \(T\) is compact, then \(T\) is weak-to-strong continuous: weakly convergent sequences are bounded, compactness gives convergent subsequences of images, and weak-weak continuity identifies every possible strong subsequential limit as \(Tx\). Conversely, if \(X\) is reflexive and \(T\) is weak-to-strong continuous, then \(T\) is compact: every bounded sequence has a weakly convergent subsequence, whose image converges strongly.

  • Let \(X, Y\) be Banach spaces with \(Y\) infinite-dimensional. Let \(K \in L (X, Y)\). Can \(K\) be both compact and surjective? Provide a detailed justification of your answer.

    Answer. No. Suppose \(K:X\to Y\) is compact and surjective, with \(Y\) infinite-dimensional. By the open mapping theorem, \(K(B_X(0,1))\) contains a ball \(B_Y(0,\delta)\). Since \(K\) is compact, \(\overline{K(B_X(0,1))}\) is compact. Therefore \(\overline{B_Y(0,\delta)}\), being a closed subset of a compact set, is compact. This contradicts Riesz theorem, because closed balls in infinite-dimensional Banach spaces are not compact. Hence a compact operator cannot be onto an infinite-dimensional Banach space.

  • Consider a linear operator \(K : X \to Y\), with \(X\) and \(Y\) Banach spaces. Write the following deﬁnitions:

  1. \(K\) is compact;
  2. \(K\) is ﬁnite rank. Which is the relation between 1. and 2.? No proofs are required.

Answer. \(K\) is compact if it maps bounded sets in \(X\) to relatively compact sets in \(Y\). Equivalently, the image of every bounded sequence has a strongly convergent subsequence. \(K\) is finite rank if \[ \dim(\operatorname{Im}K)<\infty. \] Every finite-rank continuous linear operator is compact, because bounded sets in a finite-dimensional space have compact closure. Also, norm limits of finite-rank operators are compact. The converse is false in general: not every compact operator is finite rank.

Hilbert Spaces, Riesz Representation, And Projection

  • State and prove the Riesz representation theorem in Hilbert spaces.

    Answer. Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space. For every \(\varphi\in H^*\) there exists a unique \(f\in H\) such that \[ \varphi(u)=\langle f,u\rangle\qquad\forall u\in H, \] and \(\|\varphi\|=\|f\|\). Proof: if \(\varphi=0\), take \(f=0\). Otherwise let \(M=\ker\varphi\), a closed proper subspace. Choose \(g\in M^\perp\), \(\|g\|=1\). For \(u\in H\), put \(\lambda=\varphi(u)/\varphi(g)\). Then \(u-\lambda g\in M\), hence \(\langle g,u-\lambda g\rangle=0\), so \(\langle g,u\rangle=\lambda\). Therefore \[ \varphi(u)=\varphi(g)\langle g,u\rangle=\langle \varphi(g)g,u\rangle. \] This gives the representing vector \(f=\varphi(g)g\). Uniqueness follows by testing \(\langle f_1-f_2,u\rangle=0\) with \(u=f_1-f_2\). The norm identity follows from Cauchy-Schwarz and testing with \(u=f/\|f\|\).

  • State and prove the projection theorem in Hilbert spaces.

    Answer. Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space and \(K\subset H\) nonempty, closed, convex. For every \(f\in H\), there exists a unique \(u\in K\) such that \[ \|f-u\|=\inf_{v\in K}\|f-v\|. \] Moreover \(u\) is characterized by \[ \langle f-u,v-u\rangle\le0\qquad\forall v\in K. \] Proof: take a minimizing sequence \(v_n\in K\). By the parallelogram identity and convexity, \[ \|v_n-v_m\|^2 =2\|f-v_n\|^2+2\|f-v_m\|^2-4\left\|f-\frac{v_n+v_m}{2}\right\|^2\to0. \] Thus \(v_n\) is Cauchy, so \(v_n\to u\in K\) since \(H\) is complete and \(K\) is closed. Then \(u\) minimizes. Uniqueness follows by the same parallelogram argument applied to two minimizers. For the characterization, if \(u\) minimizes, use \(u+t(v-u)\in K\), \(t\in(0,1)\), and expand \(\|f-(u+t(v-u))\|^2\ge\|f-u\|^2\), then let \(t\downarrow0\). Conversely, the inequality gives \[ \|f-v\|^2=\|f-u\|^2+\|v-u\|^2-2\langle f-u,v-u\rangle\ge\|f-u\|^2. \]

Hilbert Operators, Spectral Theory, And Solvability

  • Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space, \(T\in L(H)\). Write the following deﬁnitions:
  1. \(T\) is compact;
  2. resolvent set of \(T\);
  3. spectrum of \(T\). State the spectral theorem. No proofs are required.

Answer. \(T\) is compact if it maps bounded sets of \(H\) into relatively compact sets, equivalently if every bounded sequence \(u_n\) has a subsequence such that \(Tu_{n_k}\) converges strongly. The resolvent set is \[ \rho(T)=\{\lambda\in\mathbb K:T-\lambda I\text{ is bijective and }(T-\lambda I)^{-1}\in\mathcal L(H)\}. \] The spectrum is \[ \sigma(T)=\mathbb K\setminus\rho(T). \] Spectral theorem: if \(H\) is separable and \(T\in\mathcal K(H)\) is compact and symmetric/self-adjoint, then \(H\) has an orthonormal basis made of eigenvectors of \(T\). The corresponding eigenvalues are real, nonzero eigenvalues have finite multiplicity, and the only possible accumulation point is \(0\).

  • Let \(T : H \to H\) be a linear and bounded operator on the Hilbert space \(H\).
  1. Write the definitions of:
  1. \(T\) is symmetric;
  2. \(T\) is compact;
  3. resolvet set and spectrum of \(T\).
  1. State the spectral theorem for \(T\).

Answer. \(T\) is symmetric if \[ \langle Tx,y\rangle=\langle x,Ty\rangle\qquad\forall x,y\in H. \] \(T\) is compact if the image of every bounded set is relatively compact, equivalently every bounded sequence has an image subsequence converging strongly. The resolvent set and spectrum are \[ \rho(T)=\{\lambda:T-\lambda I\text{ is bijective with bounded inverse}\},\qquad \sigma(T)=\mathbb K\setminus\rho(T). \] If \(T\) is compact and symmetric on separable \(H\), then there is an orthonormal basis of \(H\) formed by eigenvectors of \(T\), with real eigenvalues; nonzero eigenvalues have finite multiplicity and can accumulate only at \(0\).

  • Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space and \(f \in H\). Under suitable assumptions on \(T\), that have to be specified, discuss the solvability of equation \[ u - T(u) = f . \] No proofs are required.

    Answer. Assume \(T\in\mathcal K(H)\) is compact and symmetric. By the Fredholm alternative for \(I-T\): \[ \operatorname{Im}(I-T)=\ker(I-T)^\perp, \] \(\ker(I-T)\) is finite-dimensional, and \(I-T\) is injective iff it is surjective. Therefore, if \(\ker(I-T)=\{0\}\), then for every \(f\in H\) there exists a unique solution \(u\in H\). If \(\ker(I-T)\ne\{0\}\), then the equation is solvable iff \[ f\in\ker(I-T)^\perp. \] In that case solutions are not unique: if \(u\) is one solution, then \(u+v\) is also a solution for every \(v\in\ker(I-T)\).

  • Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space, \(T : H \to H\) a linear operator, and \(f \in H\). Consider the equation \[ u - T (u) = f. \] Discuss the solvability in \(H\) of such equation, specifying appropriate assumptions on the operator \(T\). In your discussion, you are expected to use, cite and explicitly state one or more key theorems that are relevant to the analysis.

    Answer. Under the standard compact symmetric assumption \(T\in\mathcal K(H)\), \(T=T^*\), use the Fredholm alternative. It states that \(\ker(I-T)\) is finite-dimensional, \[ \operatorname{Im}(I-T)=\ker(I-T)^\perp, \] and \(I-T\) is injective iff it is surjective. Hence: if \(1\) is not an eigenvalue of \(T\), i.e. \(\ker(I-T)=\{0\}\), then \(I-T\) is bijective and the equation has a unique solution for every \(f\in H\). If \(1\) is an eigenvalue, then solutions exist exactly for \[ f\perp\ker(I-T), \] and they are unique only up to addition of elements of \(\ker(I-T)\). Equivalently, if \(u_0\) is one solution, all solutions are \(u_0+\ker(I-T)\).
