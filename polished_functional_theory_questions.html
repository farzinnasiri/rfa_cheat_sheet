1. Hahn-Banach And Separation

  1. ★★ State the Hahn-Banach theorem in the continuous extension form. Q2.42
    Answer

    Let \(X\) be a normed space over the field \(\mathbb{K}\) (where \(\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{R}\) or \(\mathbb{C}\)), \(Y\) a linear subspace of \(X\), and \(f \in Y^*\) a continuous linear functional on \(Y\). The continuous Hahn-Banach extension theorem states that there exists a continuous linear functional \(F \in X^*\) such that:

    \[ F(y) = f(y) \qquad \forall y \in Y \quad (\text{i.e., } F|_Y = f), \]
    \[ \|F\|_{X^*} = \|f\|_{Y^*}. \]
    This guarantees that every continuous linear functional defined on a subspace can be extended to the entire space without increasing its operator norm.

  2. ★★ Let \(Y\) be a vector subspace of \(X=\mathbb R^2\). Given \(f\in Y^*\), is it possible to find \(F\in X^*\) such that \(F=f\) in \(Y\) and \(\|F\|_{X^*}=\|f\|_{Y^*}\)? Q2.41
    Answer

    Yes. Since \(X = \mathbb{R}^2\) is a finite-dimensional normed space and \(Y \subset \mathbb{R}^2\) is a linear subspace, the continuous extension form of the Hahn-Banach Theorem applies directly. Any continuous linear functional \(f\) on \(Y\) has a continuous linear extension \(F \in X^*\) satisfying:

    \[ F|_Y = f \quad \text{and} \quad \|F\|_{X^*} = \|f\|_{Y^*}. \]
    Since every linear functional on a finite-dimensional space is continuous, the continuity conditions are automatically satisfied for any norm on \(\mathbb{R}^2\).

  3. ★★ State and prove the three corollaries of the Hahn-Banach Theorem. Q2.45
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: Each corollary is Hahn-Banach applied to the right one-dimensional or quotient-like subspace. Define the small functional first, compute its norm exactly, extend it, then read off separation or norm-attainment. Question focus: State and prove the three corollaries of the Hahn-Banach Theorem.

    Corollary 1: For any \(x_0 \in X\) with \(x_0 \neq 0\), there exists \(L \in X^*\) such that \(\|L\|_{X^*} = 1\) and \(L(x_0) = \|x_0\|_X\).

    Proof: Let \(Y = \operatorname{span}\{x_0\} = \{\alpha x_0 : \alpha \in \mathbb{K}\}\). Define the linear functional \(l : Y \to \mathbb{K}\) by \(l(\alpha x_0) = \alpha \|x_0\|_X\). We compute the norm of \(l\): for any \(y = \alpha x_0 \in Y\), we have \(|l(y)| = |\alpha| \|x_0\|_X = \|\alpha x_0\|_X = \|y\|_X\). Thus, \(l\) is continuous and bounded with \(\|l\|_{Y^*} = 1\). By the continuous form of the Hahn-Banach Theorem, there exists a continuous linear functional \(L \in X^*\) extending \(l\) to \(X\) such that \(L|_Y = l\) and \(\|L\|_{X^*} = \||l\|_{Y^*} = 1\). In particular, \(L(x_0) = l(x_0) = \|x_0\|_X\).

    Corollary 2: The dual space \(X^*\) separates points: if \(x, y \in X\) with \(x \neq y\), there exists \(L \in X^*\) such that \(L(x) \neq L(y)\).

    Proof: Since \(x \neq y\), we have \(x - y \neq 0\). Applying Corollary 1 to \(x_0 = x - y\), there exists \(L \in X^*\) with \(\|L\|_{X^*} = 1\) and \(L(x - y) = \|x - y\|_X \neq 0\). By linearity of \(L\), \(L(x) - L(y) \neq 0\), so \(L(x) \neq L(y)\).

    Corollary 3: Let \(Y\) be a linear subspace of a normed space \(X\). If \(x_0 \in X \setminus \overline{Y}\), then there exists \(L \in X^*\) such that \(L|_Y = 0\), \(L(x_0) = \operatorname{dist}(x_0, Y)\), and \(\|L\|_{X^*} = 1\).

    Proof: Let \(d = \operatorname{dist}(x_0, Y) = \inf_{y \in Y} \|x_0 - y\|_X\). Since \(\overline{Y}\) is closed and \(x_0 \notin \overline{Y}\), we have \(d > 0\). Define the subspace \(Z = Y + \operatorname{span}\{x_0\} = \{y + \alpha x_0 : y \in Y, \alpha \in \mathbb{K}\}\). Since \(x_0 \notin Y\), each element in \(Z\) has a unique representation. Define the linear functional \(l : Z \to \mathbb{K}\) by \(l(y + \alpha x_0) = \alpha d\). Clearly, \(l|_Y = 0\) and \(l(x_0) = d\). We show \(\|l\|_{Z^*} = 1\). For any \(z = y + \alpha x_0 \in Z\): - If \(\alpha = 0\), \(l(z) = 0 \le \|z\|_X\). - If \(\alpha \neq 0\), \(|l(z)| = |\alpha| d = |\alpha| \inf_{y' \in Y} \|x_0 - y'\|_X \le |\alpha| \|x_0 - (-y/\alpha)\|_X = \|y + \alpha x_0\|_X = \|z\|_X\). This proves \(|l(z)| \le \|z\|_X\) for all \(z \in Z\), hence \(\|l\|_{Z^*} \le 1\). By definition of the infimum, for any \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists \(y_\varepsilon \in Y\) such that \(\|x_0 - y_\varepsilon\|_X < d + \varepsilon\). Letting \(z_\varepsilon = x_0 - y_\varepsilon \in Z\), we have \(l(z_\varepsilon) = d\). Thus \(\|l\|_{Z^*} \ge \frac{|l(z_\varepsilon)|}{\|z_\varepsilon\|_X} > \frac{d}{d + \varepsilon}\). Taking \(\varepsilon \downarrow 0\) yields \(\|l\|_{Z^*} \ge 1\). Hence \(\|l\|_{Z^*} = 1\). By the Hahn-Banach Theorem, there exists \(L \in X^*\) extending \(l\) such that \(L|_Z = l\) and \(\|L\|_{X^*} = \|l\|_{Z^*} = 1\). Thus \(L|_Y = 0\) and \(L(x_0) = d = \operatorname{dist}(x_0, Y)\).

  4. ★★ Let \(X\) be a normed space, let \(H\) be a hyperplane of \(X\). When do we say that \(H\) separates or strictly separates \(A\) and \(B\)? Q2.43
    Answer

    Let \(X\) be a real normed space (or the real part of functionals if \(X\) is complex). A hyperplane \(H\) is a set of the form:

    \[ H = \{x \in X : L(x) = \alpha\}, \]
    where \(L \in X^*\) is a continuous linear functional with \(L \neq 0\) and \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\). - \(H\) **separates** two subsets \(A, B \subset X\) if:
    \[ L(a) \le \alpha \le L(b) \qquad \forall a \in A, \, \forall b \in B. \]
    - \(H\) **strictly separates** \(A\) and \(B\) if there exists \(\varepsilon > 0\) such that:
    \[ L(a) \le \alpha - \varepsilon \quad \text{and} \quad L(b) \ge \alpha + \varepsilon \qquad \forall a \in A, \, \forall b \in B. \]

  5. ★★ State the Hahn-Banach theorem in the separation form, first version. Q2.44
    Answer

    Let \(X\) be a real normed space. If \(A, B \subset X\) are nonempty disjoint convex subsets of \(X\), and \(A\) is open, then there exist a continuous linear functional \(L \in X^*\) with \(L \neq 0\) and a real number \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\) such that:

    \[ L(a) < \alpha \le L(b) \qquad \forall a \in A, \, \forall b \in B. \]
    This means the disjoint convex sets are separated by the closed hyperplane \(\{x \in X : L(x) = \alpha\}\), with the open set \(A\) lying strictly on one side.

  6. ★★ State the Hahn-Banach theorem in the separation form, second version. Q2.44
    Answer

    Let \(X\) be a real normed space. If \(A, B \subset X\) are nonempty disjoint convex subsets of \(X\), such that \(A\) is compact and \(B\) is closed, then there exist a continuous linear functional \(L \in X^*\) with \(L \neq 0\) and real numbers \(\alpha < \beta\) such that:

    \[ L(a) \le \alpha < \beta \le L(b) \qquad \forall a \in A, \, \forall b \in B. \]
    This is the strict separation theorem, establishing a positive gap between the values of the functional on \(A\) and \(B\).

2. Weak Convergence, Weak-Star Convergence, And Banach-Alaoglu

  1. ★★★ Write the definition of weak convergence. Q2.53
    Answer

    A sequence \((x_n)\) in a normed space \(X\) converges weakly to \(x \in X\), denoted by \(x_n \rightharpoonup x\), if:

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} L(x_n) = L(x) \qquad \forall L \in X^*. \]
    This means the sequence of scalars \((L(x_n))\) converges to the scalar \(L(x)\) for every continuous linear functional on \(X\).

  2. ★★★ How can weak convergence be formulated in \(L^p\) and in \(\ell^p\)? Q2.53
    Answer

    In \(L^p(X, \mu)\) spaces: - For \(1 < p < \infty\), by the Riesz Representation Theorem, the dual of \(L^p(X, \mu)\) is \(L^q(X, \mu)\) where \(\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1\). Thus, \(f_n \rightharpoonup f\) weakly in \(L^p(X, \mu)\) if:

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \int_X f_n g \, d\mu = \int_X fg \, d\mu \qquad \forall g \in L^q(X, \mu). \]
    - For \(p = 1\), on a \(\sigma\)-finite measure space, the dual is \(L^\infty(X, \mu)\). Thus, \(f_n \rightharpoonup f\) weakly in \(L^1(X, \mu)\) if:
    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \int_X f_n g \, d\mu = \int_X fg \, d\mu \qquad \forall g \in L^\infty(X, \mu). \]

    In \(\ell^p\) sequence spaces: - For \(1 \le p < \infty\), weak convergence \(x_n \rightharpoonup x\) means:

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=1}^\infty x_n(k) y(k) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty x(k) y(k) \qquad \forall y \in \ell^q, \]
    where \(\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1\) (with \(q = \infty\) when \(p=1\)). A sequence \((x_n)\) converges weakly to \(x\) in \(\ell^p\) if and only if it is norm-bounded and converges coordinatewise, i.e., \(\lim_{n\to\infty} x_n(k) = x(k)\) for each \(k \ge 1\).

  3. ★★★ Show that strong convergence implies weak convergence. Q2.54

    Since \(\|L\|_{X^*}\) is finite and \(\lim_{n\to\infty} \|x_n - x\|_X = 0\), we have \(\lim_{n\to\infty} |L(x_n) - L(x)| = 0\). This holds for all \(L \in X^*\), which proves that \(x_n \rightharpoonup x\) weakly.

  5. ★★★ Show that the weak limit is unique. Q2.55
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: To prove uniqueness, assume two weak limits and separate them. Hahn-Banach gives a functional detecting their difference, while weak convergence forces the two tested scalar limits to coincide. Question focus: Show that the weak limit is unique.

    Let \((x_n)\) be a sequence in a normed space \(X\). Suppose \(x_n \rightharpoonup x\) and \(x_n \rightharpoonup y\). Then for any continuous linear functional \(L \in X^*\), we have:

    \[ L(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} L(x_n) = L(y) \implies L(x - y) = 0. \]
    Suppose for contradiction that \(x - y \neq 0\). By a corollary of the Hahn-Banach Theorem, there exists \(L_0 \in X^*\) such that \(L_0(x - y) = \|x - y\|_X \neq 0\).   6. ★★★ If \(\{x_n\}\) weakly converges to \(x\), can \(\{x_n\}\) be unbounded? Q2.56
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: The target is boundedness from pointwise bounded scalar tests. Translate the sequence into evaluation maps on the dual, apply Uniform Boundedness, then return through the canonical embedding to bound the original norms.     No, a weakly convergent sequence is always bounded. Suppose \(x_n \rightharpoonup x\) in a normed space \(X\). Then for every \(L \in X^*\), the sequence of scalars \((L(x_n))\) is convergent, and hence bounded:

    \[ \sup_{n \ge 1} |L(x_n)| < \infty \qquad \forall L \in X^*. \]
    Let \(\tau : X \to X^{**}\) be the canonical evaluation map, where \(\tau(x_n)(L) = L(x_n)\). The dual space \(X^*\) is a Banach space. The family of linear operators \(\{\tau(x_n)\}_{n=1}^\infty \subset L(X^*, \mathbb{K})\) is pointwise bounded on \(X^*\).     \[ \sup_{n \ge 1} \|\tau(x_n)\|_{X^{**}} < \infty. \]
    Since \(\tau\) is an isometric embedding, \(\|\tau(x_n)\|_{X^{**}} = \|x_n\|_X\) for all \(n \ge 1\). Thus, \(\sup_{n \ge 1} \|x_n\|_X < \infty\).

  10. ★★★ Write the definition of weak* convergence. Q2.59
    Answer

    Let \(X\) be a normed space and \(X^*\) its dual space. A sequence \((L_n)\) in \(X^*\) converges weak-star to \(L \in X^*\) (denoted by \(L_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} L\)) if:

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} L_n(x) = L(x) \qquad \forall x \in X. \]
    Unlike weak convergence on \(X^*\) (which requires convergence when tested against all functionals in \(X^{**}\)), weak-star convergence only requires convergence when tested against the canonical images of elements of \(X\).

  11. ★★★ Write the properties of weak* convergence. Q2.61
    Answer

    Let \(X\) be a normed space. Weak-star convergence in \(X^*\) satisfies: 1. **Uniqueness of Limits:** If \(L_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} L\) and \(L_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} M\), then \(L = M\) (since the predual \(X\) separates points of \(X^*\)). 2. **Boundedness:** If \(X\) is a Banach space, then any weak-star convergent sequence \((L_n)\) in \(X^*\) is norm-bounded: \(\sup_{n\ge 1} \|L_n\|_{X^*} < \infty\) (by the Uniform Boundedness Principle). 3. **Lower Semicontinuity of the Norm:** If \(L_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} L\), then:

    \[ \|L\|_{X^*} \le \liminf_{n\to\infty} \|L_n\|_{X^*}. \]
    *Proof:* For any \(x \in X\), \(|L(x)| = \lim |L_n(x)| = \liminf |L_n(x)| \le \liminf (\|L_n\|_{X^*} \|x\|_X) = \left(\liminf \|L_n\|_{X^*}\right) \|x\|_X\). Taking the supremum over \(\|x\|_X \le 1\) yields the result. 4. **Linearity:** If \(L_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} L\) and \(M_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} M\), then \(\alpha L_n + \beta M_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} \alpha L + \beta M\) for all scalars \(\alpha, \beta\).

  12. ★★★ Which is the relation between weak and weak* convergence? Justify your answer. Q2.60
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: The proof is compactness through the correct topology. Question focus: Which is the relation between weak and weak* convergence? Justify your answer.

    Let \(X\) be a normed space and \(X^*\) its dual. Weak convergence in \(X^*\) implies weak-star convergence. The converse holds if and only if \(X\) is reflexive. - **Justification (Weak implies Weak-star):** Let \((L_n)\) be a sequence in \(X^*\) converging weakly to \(L \in X^*\). This means that for every \(\Phi \in X^{**}\), \(\lim_{n\to\infty} \Phi(L_n) = \Phi(L)\). Let \(\tau : X \to X^{**}\) be the canonical evaluation map. For any \(x \in X\), we have \(\tau(x) \in X^{**}\). Testing the weak convergence against \(\tau(x)\) gives:

    \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \tau(x)(L_n) = \tau(x)(L) \implies \lim_{n\to\infty} L_n(x) = L(x), \]
    which is the definition of weak-star convergence \(L_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} L\). - **Counterexample for the Converse (Non-reflexive case):** Let \(X = c_0\) (the space of real sequences converging to 0, with supremum norm). Its dual is \(X^* = \ell^1\), and its bidual is \(X^{**} = \ell^\infty\). Let \((e_n)_{n=1}^\infty \subset \ell^1\) be the standard basis. For any \(x \in c_0\), \(e_n(x) = x(n) \to 0\) as \(n\to\infty\). Thus \(e_n \overset{*}{\rightharpoonup} 0\) weak-star in \ell^1. However, to converge weakly to 0, it must satisfy \(\Phi(e_n) \to 0\) for all \(\Phi \in \ell^\infty\). Choosing \(y = (1, 1, 1, \dots) \in \ell^\infty\) defines a functional \(\Phi_y \in X^{**}\) such that \(\Phi_y(e_n) = y(n) = 1 \not\to 0\). Thus \((e_n)\) does not converge weakly to 0.

  13. ★★★ State the Banach-Alaoglu theorem. Q2.62
    Answer

    The closed unit ball of the dual space \(X^*\) of any normed space \(X\), defined as \(\overline{B}_{X^*} = \{ L \in X^* : \|L\|_{X^*} \le 1 \}\), is compact in the weak-star topology \(\sigma(X^*, X)\). - **Metrizability and Sequential Compactness:** If \(X\) is a separable normed space, the weak-star topology on the closed unit ball \(\overline{B}_{X^*}\) (and on any bounded subset of \(X^*\)) is metrizable. Since compact metric spaces are sequentially compact, it follows that when \(X\) is separable, any bounded sequence in \(X^*\) contains a weak-star convergent subsequence.

3. Compact Operators And Finite-Rank Operators

  1. ★★ Write the definition of compact operator. Q2.64
    Answer

    A linear operator \(T : X \to Y\) between normed spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) is **compact** if for every bounded subset \(B \subset X\), the image \(T(B)\) is relatively compact in \(Y\) (i.e., its closure \(\overline{T(B)}\) is compact in \(Y\)). Equivalently, since \(Y\) is a metric space, \(T\) is compact if and only if for every bounded sequence \((x_n) \subset X\), the sequence \((Tx_n)\) contains a subsequence that converges strongly (in norm) in \(Y\). Every compact linear operator is automatically bounded (continuous).

  2. ★★ Write the definition of operator of finite rank. Q2.64
    Answer

    A bounded linear operator \(T : X \to Y\) between normed spaces is of **finite rank** if the range of \(T\) is finite-dimensional, i.e., \(\operatorname{dim}(T(X)) < \infty\). Any bounded linear operator of finite rank can be written in the form:

    \[ Tx = \sum_{j=1}^m L_j(x) y_j \qquad \forall x \in X, \]
    where \(\{y_1, \dots, y_m\}\) is a basis of \(\operatorname{Ran}(T) \subset Y\) and \(L_1, \dots, L_m \in X^*\) are continuous linear functionals.

4. Hilbert Spaces, Fredholm, Spectrum, And Spectral Theorem

  1. ★★ State and prove the projection theorem and its corollary in Hilbert spaces. Q2.68
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: The argument is geometric. Minimize the distance to the closed convex set, use Hilbert-space parallelogram/convexity to get existence and uniqueness, then characterize the minimizer by an orthogonality inequality. Question focus: State and prove the projection theorem and its corollary in Hilbert spaces.

    Projection Theorem: Let \(K\) be a nonempty closed convex subset of a Hilbert space \(H\). For every \(x \in H\), there exists a unique \(p \in K\) such that:

    \[ \|x-p\|_2 = \inf_{y \in K} \|x-y\|_2. \]
    The point \(p\) is characterized by the variational inequality:
    \[ \operatorname{Re}\langle x-p, y-p \rangle \le 0 \qquad \forall y \in K. \]
    - *Proof:* Let \(d = \inf_{y\in K} \|x-y\|_2\). Choose a sequence \((y_n) \subset K\) such that \(\|x-y_n\|_2 \to d\). By the parallelogram identity applied to \(u = x - y_n\) and \(v = x - y_m\):
    \[ 2\|x-y_n\|_2^2 + 2\|x-y_m\|_2^2 = 4\left\|x - \frac{y_n + y_m}{2}\right\|_2^2 + \|y_n - y_m\|_2^2. \]
    Since \(K\) is convex, \(\frac{y_n + y_m}{2} \in K\), which implies \(\left\|x - \frac{y_n + y_m}{2}\right\|_2 \ge d\). Thus:
    \[ \|y_n - y_m\|_2^2 \le 2\|x-y_n\|_2^2 + 2\|x-y_m\|_2^2 - 4d^2 \to 0 \quad \text{as } n, m \to \infty. \]
    Thus \((y_n)\) is Cauchy. Since \(H\) is complete and \(K\) is closed, \(y_n \to p \in K\). By continuity of the norm, \(\|x-p\|_2 = d\). For uniqueness, if \(p_1, p_2 \in K\) satisfy \(\|x-p_1\|_2 = \|x-p_2\|_2 = d\), the same inequality yields:
    \[ \|p_1 - p_2\|_2^2 \le 2d^2 + 2d^2 - 4\left\|x - \frac{p_1 + p_2}{2}\right\|_2^2 \le 0 \implies p_1 = p_2. \]
    To prove the characterization, let \(p \in K\) satisfy the variational inequality. For any \(y \in K\):
    \[ \|x-y\|_2^2 = \|(x-p) - (y-p)\|_2^2 = \|x-p\|_2^2 + \|y-p\|_2^2 - 2 \operatorname{Re}\langle x-p, y-p \rangle \ge \|x-p\|_2^2. \]
    Conversely, if \(p\) is the projection and \(y \in K\), then by convexity, \((1-t)p + ty \in K\) for all \(t \in [0, 1]\). Thus:
    \[ \|x - p\|_2^2 \le \|x - (p + t(y-p))\|_2^2 = \|x-p\|_2^2 + t^2 \|y-p\|_2^2 - 2t \operatorname{Re}\langle x-p, y-p \rangle. \]
    Dividing by \(t > 0\) and taking \(t \downarrow 0\) yields \(\operatorname{Re}\langle x-p, y-p \rangle \le 0\).

    Corollary: If \(M\) is a closed linear subspace of \(H\), then \(H = M \oplus M^\perp\). Every \(x \in H\) can be written uniquely as \(x = m + z\) with \(m \in M\) and \(z \in M^\perp\).

    Proof: Since \(M\) is closed and convex, the projection \(m = P_M(x) \in M\) exists. Let \(z = x - m\). By the variational characterization, for any \(y \in M\) and any scalar \(\alpha \in \mathbb{K}\), since \(m + \alpha y \in M\), we have \(\operatorname{Re}\langle x - m, \alpha y \rangle \le 0\), which is \(\operatorname{Re}(\overline{\alpha}\langle z, y \rangle) \le 0\). Since \(\alpha\) is arbitrary, we choose \(\alpha = \langle z, y \rangle\) to obtain \(|\langle z, y \rangle|^2 \le 0 \implies \langle z, y \rangle = 0\) for all \(y \in M\). Thus \(z \in M^\perp\). For uniqueness, if \(x = m_1 + z_1 = m_2 + z_2\) with \(m_i \in M, z_i \in M^\perp\), then \(m_1 - m_2 = z_2 - z_1 \in M \cap M^\perp = \{0\}\). Thus \(m_1 = m_2\) and \(z_1 = z_2\).

  4. ★★ State the Fredholm alternative. Q2.74
    Answer

  8. ★★ State the spectral theorem. Q2.76
    Answer

    Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space and let \(T : H \to H\) be a compact self-adjoint operator. There exists an orthonormal system \(\{e_n\}\) of eigenvectors of \(T\) with corresponding real eigenvalues \(\{\lambda_n\}\) such that:

    This is the Riesz-Fischer theorem.

    Case 1: \(1 \le p < \infty\). Let \((f_n)\) be a Cauchy sequence in \(L^p(X, \mu)\). We extract a subsequence \((f_{n_k})\) such that \(\|f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_k}\|_p \le 2^{-k}\) for all \(k \ge 1\). Define \(g_m(x) = \sum_{k=1}^m |f_{n_{k+1}}(x) - f_{n_k}(x)|\) and \(g(x) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty |f_{n_{k+1}}(x) - f_{n_k}(x)|\). By Minkowski's inequality, \(\|g_m\|_p \le \sum_{k=1}^m \|f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_k}\|_p \le \sum_{k=1}^m 2^{-k} < 1\). Applying the Monotone Convergence Theorem to \((g_m^p)\):

    \[ \int_X g^p \, d\mu = \lim_{m\to\infty} \int_X g_m^p \, d\mu \le 1. \]
    Thus \(g \in L^p(X, \mu)\), which implies \(g(x) < \infty\) for a.e. \(x \in X\). This means the telescopic series \(f_{n_1}(x) + \sum_{k=1}^\infty (f_{n_{k+1}}(x) - f_{n_k}(x))\) converges absolutely for a.e. \(x \in X\). Let \(f(x)\) be the pointwise limit of the subsequence \(f_{n_k}(x)\) (set \(f(x) = 0\) where it diverges). To show \(f_{n_k} \to f\) in \(L^p\), apply Fatou's Lemma with respect to \(j \to \infty\):
    \[ \int_X |f_{n_k} - f|^p \, d\mu = \int_X \liminf_{j\to\infty} |f_{n_k} - f_{n_j}|^p \, d\mu \le \liminf_{j\to\infty} \int_X |f_{n_k} - f_{n_j}|^p \, d\mu. \]
    Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), choose \(N\) such that for all \(k, j \ge N\), \(\|f_{n_k} - f_{n_j}\|_p \le \varepsilon\). Then for \(n_k \ge N\), we have \(\|f_{n_k} - f\|_p \le \varepsilon\). Thus \(f - f_{n_k} \in L^p\), so \(f \in L^p\), and \(\|f_{n_k} - f\|_p \to 0\). Since the original sequence is Cauchy and has a convergent subsequence, the entire sequence converges to \(f\) in \(L^p(X, \mu)\).

    Case 2: \(p = \infty\). Let \((f_n)\) be a Cauchy sequence in \(L^\infty(X, \mu)\). For each \(n, m \ge 1\), let \(E_{n,m} = \{ x \in X : |f_n(x) - f_m(x)| > \|f_n - f_m\|_\infty \}\). By definition of the essential supremum, \(\mu(E_{n,m}) = 0\). Let \(E = \bigcup_{n,m=1}^\infty E_{n,m}\). Since \(E\) is a countable union of null sets, \(\mu(E) = 0\). For any \(x \notin E\), we have \(|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \le \|f_n - f_m\|_\infty\) for all \(n, m \ge 1\). This means that for every \(x \notin E\), \((f_n(x))\) is a Cauchy sequence of scalars. Let \(f(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x)\) for \(x \notin E\), and \(f(x) = 0\) for \(x \in E\). Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), there is \(N\) such that for all \(n, m \ge N\), \(\|f_n - f_m\|_\infty \le \varepsilon\). Thus for all \(x \notin E\) and \(n, m \ge N\), \(|f_n(x) - f_m(x)| \le \varepsilon\). Taking \(m \to \infty\) yields \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| \le \varepsilon\) for all \(x \notin E\) and \(n \ge N\). This implies \(\|f_n - f\|_\infty \le \varepsilon\) for all \(n \ge N\), showing \(f_n \to f\) in \(L^\infty(X, \mu)\).

    Theorem: If \(\mu(X) < \infty\) and \(1 \le p \le q \le \infty\), then \(L^q(X, \mu) \subset L^p(X, \mu)\), and:

    \[ \|f\|_p \le \mu(X)^{1/p - 1/q} \|f\|_q. \]

    \[ \int_X |f|^p \cdot 1 \, d\mu \le \left( \int_X (|f|^p)^{q/p} \, d\mu \right)^{p/q} \left( \int_X 1^{\frac{q}{q-p}} \, d\mu \right)^{1 - p/q} = \|f\|_q^p \cdot \mu(X)^{1 - p/q}. \]
    Taking the \(1/p\)-th power of both sides yields \(\|f\|_p \le \mu(X)^{1/p - 1/q} \|f\|_q\). The **finite-measure hypothesis** \(\mu(X) < \infty\) is essential. On infinite-measure spaces, there are no general inclusions. For example, on \(X = (0, \infty)\) with Lebesgue measure, \(f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} \chi_{(0,1)}(x)\) is in \(L^1\) but not in \(L^2\), while \(g(x) = \frac{1}{x} \chi_{(1,\infty)}(x)\) is in \(L^2\) but not in \(L^1\).

    Answer

    Interpolation Inequality: Let \(1 \le p < r < q \le \infty\) and choose \(\theta \in (0, 1)\) such that \(\frac{1}{r} = \frac{\theta}{p} + \frac{1-\theta}{q}\). If \(f \in L^p(X, \mu) \cap L^q(X, \mu)\), then \(f \in L^r(X, \mu)\) and:

    \[ \|f\|_r \le \|f\|_p^\theta \|f\|_q^{1-\theta}. \]

    \[ \int_X |f|^r \, d\mu \le \left( \int_X |f|^{\theta r u} \, d\mu \right)^{1/u} \left( \int_X |f|^{(1-\theta)r v} \, d\mu \right)^{1/v} = \left( \int_X |f|^p \, d\mu \right)^{\frac{\theta r}{p}} \left( \int_X |f|^q \, d\mu \right)^{\frac{(1-\theta)r}{q}}. \]
    Taking the \(1/r\)-th power yields \(\|f\|_r \le \|f\|_p^\theta \|f\|_q^{1-\theta}\). If \(q = \infty\), we have \(\theta = p/r\). Write \(\int_X |f|^r \, d\mu \le \|f\|_\infty^{r-p} \int_X |f|^p \, d\mu = \|f\|_\infty^{r-p} \|f\|_p^p\). Taking the \(1/r\)-th power yields \(\|f\|_r \le \|f\|_p^{p/r} \|f\|_\infty^{1 - p/r} = \|f\|_p^\theta \|f\|_\infty^{1-\theta}\).

    The sequence space \(\ell^p\) is a special case of \(L^p(X, \mathcal{A}, \mu)\) where the underlying measure space is \(X = \mathbb{N}\), \(\mathcal{A} = \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})\) is the discrete \(\sigma\)-algebra, and \(\mu = \#\) is the counting measure.   9. ★★ What is the inclusion of \(\ell^p\) spaces? Q2.25
    Answer

    For sequence spaces, if \(1 \le p \le q \le \infty\), then \(\ell^p \subset \ell^q\), and:

    \[ \|x\|_q \le \|x\|_p. \]
    *Proof:* If \(x = 0\), the inequality is trivial. If \(x \neq 0\), assume \(\|x\|_p = 1\) by scaling. Then \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty |x_k|^p = 1\), which implies \(|x_k| \le 1\) for all \(k \ge 1\). Since \(q \ge p\), we have \(|x_k|^q \le |x_k|^p\) for all \(k\). Thus \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty |x_k|^q \le \sum_{k=1}^\infty |x_k|^p = 1\). Taking the \(1/q\)-th power yields \(\|x\|_q \le 1 = \|x\|_p\). By scaling, \(\|x\|_q \le \|x\|_p\) holds generally. This inclusion direction is opposite to that of \(L^p\) spaces on finite measure spaces because counting measure has infinite total mass, but its atoms have a positive lower bound (measure 1), forcing sequences to decay to 0 and thus remain bounded.

  10. ★★ For which \(p\) are \(L^p(\mathbb R)\) and \(\ell^p\) separable? Q2.23, Q2.24, Q2.25

    Both \(L^p(\mathbb{R})\) and \(\ell^p\) are separable for \(1 \le p < \infty\). - In \(\ell^p\), the set of sequences with finite support and rational values is countable and dense. - In \(L^p(\mathbb{R})\), the set of simple functions of the form \(\sum_{j=1}^m q_j \chi_{I_j}\) where \(q_j \in \mathbb{Q}\) and \(I_j\) are intervals with rational endpoints is countable and dense. The spaces \(L^\infty(\mathbb{R})\) and \(\ell^\infty\) are **not separable**. - *Proof for \(\ell^\infty\):* For any subset \(S \subset \mathbb{N}\), let \(\chi_S \in \ell^\infty\) be its characteristic sequence. The set of all subsets \(\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})\) is uncountable, so there are uncountably many such sequences in \(\ell^\infty\). For each \(S \subset \mathbb{N}\), the open ball \(B(\chi_S, 1/3)\) must contain an element of \(D\).   1. ★ Write the definition of separable metric space and show that \(C^0([a,b])\) is separable. Q2.2, Q2.46
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: The task wants an explicit countable dense set. Define separability, then approximate continuous functions uniformly by polynomials and restrict to polynomials with rational coefficients. Question focus: Write the definition of separable metric space and show that \(C^0([a,b])\) is separable.

    A metric space \(X\) is **separable** if it contains a countable, dense subset. That is, there exists a countable subset \(D \subset X\) such that \(\overline{D} = X\).

    We show that the space \(C^0([a, b])\) of continuous, real-valued functions on the compact interval \([a, b]\), equipped with the supremum norm \(\|f\|_\infty = \max_{x \in [a, b]} |f(x)|\), is separable by proving that the set of polynomials with rational coefficients, \(\mathbb{Q}[x]\), is countable and dense in \(C^0([a, b])\).

    Countability: Every polynomial \(P \in \mathbb{Q}[x]\) can be written as \(P(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n q_k x^k\) for some \(n \in \mathbb{N}\) and \(q_k \in \mathbb{Q}\). For each fixed \(n\), the set of polynomials of degree at most \(n\) with rational coefficients is in bijection with \(\mathbb{Q}^{n+1}\). Since \(\mathbb{Q}\) is countable, \(\mathbb{Q}^{n+1}\) is countable for every \(n\). The set of all polynomials with rational coefficients is the countable union:

    \[ \mathbb{Q}[x] = \bigcup_{n=0}^\infty \mathbb{Q}^{n+1}, \]
    which is countable because a countable union of countable sets is countable.

    Density: Let \(f \in C^0([a, b])\) and let \(\varepsilon > 0\). By the Weierstrass Approximation Theorem, there exists a polynomial \(P(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n c_k x^k\) with real coefficients \(c_k \in \mathbb{R}\) such that:

    \[ \|f - P\|_\infty < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}. \]
    Let \(M = \max\{|a|, |b|, 1\}\). Since \(\mathbb{Q}\) is dense in \(\mathbb{R}\), we can choose rational numbers \(q_k \in \mathbb{Q}\) for each \(k = 0, 1, \dots, n\) such that:
    \[ |c_k - q_k| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2(n+1)M^n}. \]
    Define the rational polynomial \(Q(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n q_k x^k \in \mathbb{Q}[x]\). For any \(x \in [a, b]\):
    \[ |P(x) - Q(x)| = \left| \sum_{k=0}^n (c_k - q_k) x^k \right| \le \sum_{k=0}^n |c_k - q_k| |x|^k \le \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{\varepsilon}{2(n+1)M^n} M^n = \frac{\varepsilon}{2}. \]
    Taking the supremum over \(x \in [a, b]\) yields \(\|P - Q\|_\infty \le \frac{\varepsilon}{2}\). By the triangle inequality:
    \[ \|f - Q\|_\infty \le \|f - P\|_\infty + \|P - Q\|_\infty < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon. \]
    Since for any \(f \in C^0([a, b])\) and any \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists \(Q \in \mathbb{Q}[x]\) with \(\|f - Q\|_\infty < \varepsilon\), the countable set \(\mathbb{Q}[x]\) is dense in \(C^0([a, b])\). Hence, \(C^0([a, b])\) is separable.

  2. ★ State and prove the Banach-Steinhaus theorem, also called the Uniform Boundedness Principle. Q2.31
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: The proof uses Baire category to upgrade pointwise boundedness to uniform boundedness. Build closed sets where the operator family is bounded, find one with interior, then use linearity and scaling to get a global bound. Question focus: State and prove the Banach-Steinhaus theorem, also called the Uniform Boundedness Principle.

    Uniform Boundedness Principle: Let \(X\) be a Banach space, \(Y\) a normed space, and \(\mathcal{F} \subset L(X, Y)\). If \(\mathcal{F}\) is pointwise bounded, i.e.,

    \[ \sup_{T \in \mathcal{F}} \|Tx\|_Y < \infty \qquad \forall x \in X, \]
    then \(\mathcal{F}\) is uniformly bounded in operator norm:
    \[ \sup_{T \in \mathcal{F}} \|T\|_{L(X,Y)} < \infty. \]
    Proof: For each \(n \ge 1\), define the set \(E_n = \{ x \in X : \|Tx\|_Y \le n \quad \forall T \in \mathcal{F} \} = \bigcap_{T \in \mathcal{F}} \{ x \in X : \|Tx\|_Y \le n \}\). Since each \(T \in \mathcal{F}\) is continuous, the set \(\{ x \in X : \|Tx\|_Y \le n \}\) is closed (preimage of the closed interval \([0, n]\)). Thus, \(E_n\) is closed. By pointwise boundedness, for every \(x \in X\), there is \(n\) such that \(\sup_{T\in\mathcal{F}} \|Tx\|_Y \le n\). Thus \(X = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_n\). Since \(X\) is complete, the Baire Category Theorem implies that at least one \(E_N\) must have non-empty interior. Thus, there exist \(x_0 \in X\) and \(r > 0\) such that the closed ball \(\overline{B}(x_0, r) = \{ x \in X : \|x-x_0\|_X \le r \} \subset E_N\). For any \(T \in \mathcal{F}\) and any \(h \in X\) with \(\|h\|_X \le r\), we have \(x_0 + h \in \overline{B}(x_0, r) \subset E_N\). By linearity of \(T\):
    \[ \|Th\|_Y = \|T(x_0+h) - Tx_0\|_Y \le \|T(x_0+h)\|_Y + \|Tx_0\|_Y \le 2N. \]
    For any non-zero \(x \in X\), letting \(h = \frac{r}{\|x\|_X} x\), we have \(\|h\|_X = r\). Thus:
    \[ \|T x\|_Y = \frac{\|x\|_X}{r} \|Th\|_Y \le \frac{2N}{r} \|x\|_X. \]
    This holds for all \(x \in X\) and all \(T \in \mathcal{F}\), so \(\sup_{T \in \mathcal{F}} \|T\|_{L(X,Y)} \le \frac{2N}{r} < \infty\).

  4. ★ Define the evaluation map, also called the canonical map, list its main properties, and give the definition of reflexive Banach space. Q2.49, Q2.50
    Answer

    Let \(X\) be a normed space. The **canonical evaluation map** is the mapping \(\tau : X \to X^{**}\) defined by:

    \[ \tau(x)(L) = L(x) \qquad \forall L \in X^*. \]
    Main Properties: - \(\tau\) is linear: \(\tau(\alpha x + \beta y)(L) = L(\alpha x + \beta y) = \alpha L(x) + \beta L(y) = \alpha \tau(x)(L) + \beta \tau(y)(L)\). - \(\tau\) is an isometry: \(\|\tau(x)\|_{X^{**}} = \|x\|_X\). Indeed, by definition, \(\|\tau(x)\|_{X^{**}} = \sup_{\|L\|_{X^*} \le 1} |L(x)|\). By a corollary of the Hahn-Banach theorem, there exists \(L \in X^*\) with \(\|L\|_{X^*} = 1\) and \(L(x) = \|x\|_X\). Thus, the supremum is exactly \(\|x\|_X\). A Banach space \(X\) is **reflexive** if the canonical evaluation map \(\tau : X \to X^{**}\) is surjective (and thus is an isometric isomorphism between \(X\) and \(X^{**}\)).

  5. ★ State and prove the Riesz representation theorem in Hilbert spaces. Q2.69
    Answer

    Proof sketch / Polya view: Separate the zero functional from the nonzero case. For a nonzero functional, decompose the Hilbert space into kernel plus its orthogonal direction, choose the representing vector on that direction, and verify uniqueness by testing inner products. Question focus: State and prove the Riesz representation theorem in Hilbert spaces.

    Riesz Representation Theorem: Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space. For every continuous linear functional \(F \in H^*\), there exists a unique \(y \in H\) such that:

    \[ F(x) = \langle x, y \rangle \qquad \forall x \in H. \]
    Furthermore, we have the norm identity \(\|F\|_{H^*} = \|y\|_H\).

    Proof: Uniqueness: Suppose there exist \(y_1, y_2 \in H\) such that \(F(x) = \langle x, y_1 \rangle = \langle x, y_2 \rangle\) for all \(x \in H\). Then \(\langle x, y_1 - y_2 \rangle = 0\) for all \(x \in H\). Choosing \(x = y_1 - y_2\) yields \(\|y_1 - y_2\|_H^2 = 0 \implies y_1 = y_2\).

    Existence: If \(F = 0\), we choose \(y = 0\). Assume \(F \neq 0\). Let \(M = \ker F = \{x \in H : F(x) = 0\}\). Since \(F\) is continuous and linear, \(M\) is a closed proper subspace of \(H\). By the Projection Theorem Corollary, \(H = M \oplus M^\perp\). Since \(M\) is a proper subspace, \(M^\perp \neq \{0\}\). Choose a non-zero vector \(z_0 \in M^\perp\). Since \(z_0 \notin M\), we have \(F(z_0) \neq 0\). For any \(x \in H\), define the vector:

    \[ u = x - \frac{F(x)}{F(z_0)} z_0. \]
    By linearity, \(F(u) = F(x) - \frac{F(x)}{F(z_0)} F(z_0) = 0\), so \(u \in M\). Since \(z_0 \in M^\perp\), \(u\) is orthogonal to \(z_0\):
    \[ 0 = \langle u, z_0 \rangle = \left\langle x - \frac{F(x)}{F(z_0)} z_0, z_0 \right\rangle = \langle x, z_0 \rangle - \frac{F(x)}{F(z_0)} \|z_0\|_H^2. \]
    Solving for \(F(x)\) gives:
    \[ F(x) = \left\langle x, \frac{\overline{F(z_0)}}{\|z_0\|_H^2} z_0 \right\rangle. \]
    Thus, choosing \(y = \frac{\overline{F(z_0)}}{\|z_0\|_H^2} z_0 \in H\) gives \(F(x) = \langle x, y \rangle\) for all \(x \in H\).

    Norm Identity: If \(y = 0\), then \(F = 0\) and \(\|F\|_{H^*} = 0 = \|y\|_H\). If \(y \neq 0\), then by Cauchy-Schwarz:

    \[ |F(x)| = |\langle x, y \rangle| \le \|x\|_H \|y\|_H \implies \|F\|_{H^*} = \sup_{x \neq 0} \frac{|F(x)|}{\|x\|_H} \le \|y\|_H. \]
    On the other hand, evaluating the functional at \(x = y\) gives:
    \[ |F(y)| = \langle y, y \rangle = \|y\|_H^2 \implies \|F\|_{H^*} \ge \frac{|F(y)|}{\|y\|_H} = \|y\|_H. \]
    Combining these inequalities yields \(\|F\|_{H^*} = \|y\|_H\).
