Exercises Cheat Sheet

1. First Moves In Exercise Solutions

  1. Start by naming the space and norm. Every boundedness, compactness, and convergence claim depends on the target norm.
  2. For a linear operator, continuity means boundedness. Write: since \(T\) is linear, it is continuous if and only if it is bounded.
  3. To prove boundedness, estimate the output norm by the input norm. Aim for \(\|Tx\|_Y \le C\|x\|_X\).
  4. To compute the operator norm, prove both inequalities. First show \(\|T\|\le C\), then test a special vector/function to get \(\|T\|\ge C\).
  5. When the exact norm is hard, look for equality cases. Use constants, basis vectors, signs, or functions supported where the kernel is largest.
  6. For a sequence problem, find the pointwise or coordinatewise limit first. It is usually the only possible strong, weak, or weak-star limit.
  7. If strong convergence is claimed, compute the norm of the difference. Do not rely only on pointwise convergence.
  8. If convergence fails, produce one stable obstruction. A nonzero norm, a nonintegrable candidate limit, or one bad functional is enough.
  9. Use the shortest theorem that fits the data. DCT needs an integrable dominator; Fatou needs nonnegative functions; Arzela-Ascoli needs boundedness and equicontinuity.

2. Pointwise, A.E., And Measurability

  1. Split the domain when the formula changes. Indicator functions, truncations, and moving supports usually require separate regions.
  2. For fixed \(x\), eventually decide whether \(x\) lies in the support. This is the cleanest way to handle \(\chi_{[0,n]}\), \(\chi_{[n-1,n+1]}\), and similar terms.
  3. A.E. convergence ignores null exceptional sets. If failure happens only at one point, say pointwise convergence may fail there but a.e. convergence still holds.
  4. Measurability is often inherited. Sums, products, quotients with nonzero denominator, powers, continuous compositions, and indicators of measurable sets are measurable.
  5. For piecewise functions, check measurable pieces and measurable sets. Then conclude the whole function is measurable.
  6. For positive set functions, prove \(\mu(\emptyset)=0\) and countable additivity. If \(\mu\) is a weighted sum of known measures, use additivity term by term.
  7. To prove finite measure, bound \(\mu(E)\) by \(\mu(X)\). Then show the total mass is finite.
  8. Finite measure implies sigma-finite. Write: since \(\mu(X)<\infty\), the space is sigma-finite.
  9. For absolute continuity of measures, test null sets. Show \(\lambda(E)=0\Rightarrow \mu(E)=0\), or find a null set with positive \(\mu\)-mass.

3. \(L^p\) And \(L^\infty\) Convergence

  1. To prove \(L^p\) convergence, apply DCT to \(|f_n-f|^p\). You need a.e. convergence to \(0\) and an \(L^1\) dominating function.
  2. For \(L^1\) convergence, apply DCT directly to \(|f_n-f|\). The dominator must be integrable on the exact domain.
  3. On finite intervals, bounded pointwise estimates are powerful. A bounded interval often turns a bounded dominator into an \(L^1\) dominator.
  4. On infinite intervals, check tail integrability. Compare with \(x^{-\alpha}\), exponentials, or a known integrable envelope.
  5. If the candidate \(L^1\) limit is not in \(L^1\), convergence in \(L^1\) is impossible. \(L^1\) is complete and its norm limit must belong to \(L^1\).
  6. Fatou disproves \(L^1\) convergence for positive functions. If \(\int f=+\infty\), then \(\liminf\int f_n\ge\int f=+\infty\).
  7. \(L^p\) convergence implies convergence in measure. Use Chebyshev: \(\mu(|f_n-f|>\varepsilon)\le \|f_n-f\|_p^p/\varepsilon^p\).
  8. In \(L^\infty\), compute the essential supremum. A.E. convergence does not imply \(\|f_n-f\|_\infty\to0\).
  9. To disprove \(L^\infty\) convergence, find points or sets where the error remains large. It is enough to show \(\|f_n-f\|_\infty\ge c>0\).
  10. When support moves to infinity, \(L^p\) norm may stay constant. This often gives weak convergence without strong convergence.
  11. When support shrinks, compute the area or length. Norm convergence may follow from vanishing measure of support.
  12. For integrals with parameters, isolate a dominating function independent of \(n\). Then DCT gives limits of integrals and norms.

4. Weak And Weak-Star Convergence

  1. Weak convergence means testing against every continuous linear functional. Write \(L(x_n)\to L(x)\) for all \(L\in X^*\).
  2. Strong convergence implies weak convergence. Use \(|L(x_n)-L(x)|\le \|L\|\|x_n-x\|\).
  3. Weak limits are unique. If two limits existed, Hahn-Banach separates their difference.
  4. Weak convergence implies boundedness. The usual proof uses Banach-Steinhaus on the canonical images in \(X^{**}\).
  5. In \(\ell^p\), weak convergence implies coordinatewise convergence. Test against coordinate functionals.
  6. For \(1 Use the dual \(\ell^q\) and tail estimates.
  7. In \(\ell^2\), use Riesz representation. Test \(x_n\) by inner products \(\langle x_n,z\rangle\) with \(z\in\ell^2\).
  8. The basis vectors \(e_n\) are weakly null in \(\ell^p\), \(1 But \(\|e_n\|_p=1\), so they do not converge strongly.
  9. Weak convergence in \(\ell^1\) is stricter. Coordinatewise convergence alone is not enough; test with a bounded sequence in \(\ell^\infty\).
  10. To disprove weak convergence, find one functional whose scalar sequence does not converge to the claimed value. One counter-test is enough.
  11. In \(L^2\), test weak convergence with \(g\in L^2\). Estimate \(\int f_n g\) by Cauchy-Schwarz or Holder on the support of \(f_n\).
  12. Moving bump functions can be weakly null in \(L^2\). Their support escapes, so \(\int_{\operatorname{supp} f_n}|g|^2\to0\) for each fixed \(g\in L^2\).
  13. If the strong norm stays constant, weak convergence cannot be upgraded to strong convergence. State the norm calculation explicitly.
  14. Weak-star convergence in \(\ell^\infty=(\ell^1)^*\) is tested by \(\ell^1\) sequences. Show \(\sum_k x_n(k)y(k)\to\sum_k x(k)y(k)\) for every \(y\in\ell^1\).
  15. For weak-star proofs in \(\ell^\infty\), split into a finite head and a small tail. Use pointwise convergence on finitely many coordinates and \(\ell^1\)-smallness on the tail.

5. Compact Operators

  1. Definition sentence: \(T\) is compact if it maps bounded sets into relatively compact sets.
  2. Sequential sentence: from every bounded sequence \((x_n)\), the sequence \((Tx_n)\) has a convergent subsequence.
  3. Finite-rank operators are compact. Their image of a bounded set lives in a bounded subset of a finite-dimensional space.
  4. Norm limits of compact operators are compact. This is the standard closure argument for truncation proofs.
  5. For sequence-space operators, try finite-rank truncations. Define \(T_N\) by keeping the first \(N\) coordinates and setting the rest to zero.
  6. After defining \(T_N\), prove \(\|T-T_N\|\to0\). Usually this is a tail estimate from a convergent series or a vanishing coefficient.
  7. For integral operators into \(C([a,b])\), try Arzela-Ascoli. Show the image of each bounded set is uniformly bounded and equicontinuous.
  8. Equiboundedness usually comes from the boundedness estimate. If \(\|u\|\le R\), then \(\|Tu\|_\infty\le CR\).
  9. Equicontinuity usually comes from estimating \(|Tu(x)-Tu(y)|\). Make the bound go to \(0\) as \(|x-y|\to0\), uniformly over the bounded set.
  10. If \(Tu(x)=\int_a^x k(t)u(t)dt\), use absolute continuity of the integral. Small intervals make \(\int_x^y |k(t)|dt\) small uniformly in \(u\).
  11. If the kernel is bounded, use length times sup norm. \(\int_x^y |k(t)u(t)|dt\le \|k\|_\infty R |x-y|\).
  12. Compact plus surjective onto an infinite-dimensional Banach space is impossible. The unit ball would become compact, contradicting Riesz lemma.
  13. A bijective compact operator on an infinite-dimensional Banach space is impossible. If the inverse were bounded, the identity would be compact.
  14. To disprove compactness, find a bounded sequence whose images have no convergent subsequence. A separated sequence is ideal.
  15. Separated-image sentence: if \(\|Tx_i-Tx_j\|\ge c>0\) for \(i\ne j\), then \((Tx_i)\) has no Cauchy subsequence.
  16. Basis vectors are standard compactness tests. Compute \(Te_n\) or \(Te_{2n}\) and check whether the images stay separated.

6. Injectivity, Surjectivity, Range, And Eigenvalues

  1. Injectivity means \(\ker T=\{0\}\). Solve \(Tx=0\), not \(Tx=Ty\), unless the latter is simpler.
  2. To prove not injective, exhibit one nonzero element in the kernel. A function with zero integral on required intervals is often enough.
  3. For integral operators \(Tf(x)=\int_a^x f\), constants in the range must satisfy boundary conditions. If every \(Tf\) has \(Tf(a)=0\), the constant \(1\) is not in the range.
  4. To disprove surjectivity, find a structural property of every \(Tu\). Examples: \(Tu(0)=0\), \(Tu\) is absolutely continuous, or \(Tu\) has forced jumps/constancy.
  5. Compactness can disprove surjectivity. If the codomain is infinite-dimensional Banach and \(T\) is compact, then \(T\) cannot be onto.
  6. To prove surjectivity directly, solve \(Tx=y\). Then verify the constructed \(x\) belongs to the domain.
  7. For difference operators, recover \(x\) recursively from \(y\). Finish by checking \(x\in\ell^p\).
  8. \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue iff \(\ker(T-\lambda I)\ne\{0\}\). Always translate the question into this kernel equation.
  9. For \(\lambda=0\), eigenvalue means \(T\) is not injective. Any nonzero kernel vector is an eigenvector.
  10. For sequence operators, solve the eigenvalue recurrence. The recurrence may produce a sequence outside \(\ell^p\), which disproves eigenvalue status.
  11. For multiplication operators, eigenvectors live where the multiplier equals \(\lambda\). If that set has positive measure, \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue.
  12. For spectrum exclusions, use the norm bound. If \(T\in L(X)\), then \(\sigma(T)\subset\{\lambda:|\lambda|\le\|T\|\}\).
  13. If \(|\lambda|>\|T\|\), then \(\lambda\notin\sigma(T)\). Say this before trying to solve the resolvent by hand.

7. Absolute Continuity, BV, And Weak Derivatives

  1. On \((0,1]\), classical differentiability is not enough for absolute continuity on \([0,1]\). The behavior near \(0\) is usually decisive.
  2. Absolutely continuous functions are continuous and have \(L^1\) derivative. Failure of continuity or integrability rules out AC.
  3. If \(f\in AC([a,b])\), then \(f(x)=f(a)+\int_a^x f'(t)dt\). Use this identity to verify or disprove AC.
  4. For \(W^{1,1}\), look for an \(L^1\) weak derivative. If the a.e. derivative is in \(L^1\) and the function is AC on compact subintervals, this often works.
  5. When \(f\) is AC on every \([\varepsilon,1]\), compute the weak derivative there first. Then check integrability near \(0\).
  6. Weak derivative often equals the classical derivative a.e. State this only after verifying the Sobolev/AC conditions needed in the exercise.
  7. A jump discontinuity creates a distributional Dirac term. Such a function is not in \(W^{1,1}\) if the derivative would need a measure.
  8. For bounded variation, use partitions that follow oscillations. If sums of jumps or oscillations diverge, the function is not BV.
  9. To prove not BV, build a partition with variation growing without bound. You do not need to compute the exact total variation.
  10. For an integral operator \(Tf(x)=\int_a^x h(t)f(t)dt\), \(Tf\) is absolutely continuous. Its a.e. derivative is \(h(x)f(x)\).

8. Standard Estimates To Reuse

  1. Triangle estimate: \(|a-b|\le |a|+|b|\); use it for difference operators on \(\ell^p\).
  2. Integral sup estimate: \(\left|\int_A f\right|\le\int_A |f|\le \|f\|_\infty \lambda(A)\).
  3. Holder estimate: \(\int_A |fg|\le \|f\|_{L^p(A)}\|g\|_{L^q(A)}\).
  4. Cauchy-Schwarz on moving supports: \(\int_{A_n}|g|\le \lambda(A_n)^{1/2}\left(\int_{A_n}|g|^2\right)^{1/2}\).
  5. Tail of \(L^p\) functions: if \(g\in L^p\), then \(\int_{|x|>R}|g|^p\to0\).
  6. Tail of \(\ell^p\) sequences: if \(z\in\ell^p\), then \(\sum_{k>N}|z(k)|^p\to0\).
  7. Series comparison: \(\sum k^{-\alpha}\) converges iff \(\alpha>1\).
  8. Geometric tails vanish. Use this for compactness proofs into \(\ell^1\) with weights like \(2^{-k}\).
  9. Coordinate functionals are continuous on \(\ell^p\). They are the fastest way to identify possible weak limits.
  10. Boundedness plus pointwise convergence can imply weak convergence in reflexive sequence spaces. But always name the duality being used.
  11. When testing \(L^2\) weak convergence, localize the integral. Split into the support of \(f_n\), then use absolute continuity of the integral of \(|g|^2\).
  12. For DCT, write the three checks explicitly. Measurability, a.e. convergence, and domination by one \(L^1\) function.
