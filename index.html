Question 1.20. Write the definition of measurable function. State the theorem about the measurability of the composite function.

Exam Answer

Definition (measurable function).
Let \((X,\mathcal{A})\), \((X',\mathcal{A}')\) be measurable spaces. A map \(f:X\to X'\) is measurable iff \[ \forall E\in\mathcal{A}',\quad f^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal{A}. \]

Theorem (measurability of composition).
Let \((X,\mathcal{A})\), \((X',\mathcal{A}')\), \((X'',\mathcal{A}'')\) be measurable spaces.
If \(f:X\to X'\) is \((\mathcal{A},\mathcal{A}')\)-measurable and \(g:X'\to X''\) is \((\mathcal{A}',\mathcal{A}'')\)-measurable, then \(g\circ f:X\to X''\) is \((\mathcal{A},\mathcal{A}'')\)-measurable.

Proof.
Take \(E\in\mathcal{A}''\). Since \(g\) is measurable, \(g^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal{A}'\). Since \(f\) is measurable, \[ f^{-1}(g^{-1}(E))\in\mathcal{A}. \] But \[ (g\circ f)^{-1}(E)=f^{-1}(g^{-1}(E)), \] hence \((g\circ f)^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal{A}\), so \(g\circ f\) is measurable.

Real-analysis corollary.
If \(f:X\to\mathbb{R}\) is Lebesgue measurable and \(g:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) is continuous, then \(g\circ f\) is Lebesgue measurable.

Caveat.
In general, composition of two \(L\)-measurable functions need not be \(L\)-measurable (the middle \(\sigma\)-algebra issue).

Question 1.21. Write the characterization of measurability of functions.

Exam Answer

Characterization theorem.
Let \((X,\mathcal{A})\) be a measurable space and \(f:X\to\overline{\mathbb{R}}=[-\infty,+\infty]\). The following are equivalent: 1. \(f\) is measurable. 2. For every \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), \(\{x\in X: f(x)>\alpha\}\in\mathcal{A}\). 3. For every \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), \(\{x\in X: f(x)\ge \alpha\}\in\mathcal{A}\). 4. For every \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), \(\{x\in X: f(x)<\alpha\}\in\mathcal{A}\). 5. For every \(\alpha\in\mathbb{R}\), \(\{x\in X: f(x)\le \alpha\}\in\mathcal{A}\).

Mechanism.
The rays \((-\infty,\alpha)\), \((-\infty,\alpha]\), \((\alpha,\infty)\), \([\alpha,\infty)\) generate the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra, and preimages preserve complements, countable unions, and countable intersections.

Question 1.26. Establish all equivalent statements to the fact thatf:X→ R is measurable.

Exam Answer

Let \((X,\mathcal A)\) be a measurable space and \(f:X\to\overline{\mathbb R}\). The following are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) is measurable.
  2. \(\{f>\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\in\mathbb R\).
  3. \(\{f\ge\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\in\mathbb R\).
  4. \(\{f<\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\in\mathbb R\).
  5. \(\{f\le\alpha\}\in\mathcal A\) for every \(\alpha\in\mathbb R\).

The proof uses complements and countable unions/intersections, for instance \[ \{f\ge\alpha\}=\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty \{f>\alpha-1/n\}, \qquad \{f<\alpha\}=\{f\ge\alpha\}^c. \] Rays generate \(\mathcal B(\overline{\mathbb R})\), so any one of these conditions characterizes measurability.

Question 1.28. Let{fn}⊂M (X,A). Show that supnfn, inf nfn, lim supnfn, lim infnfn∈ (X,A). Can they exist two functionsf,g ∈M (X,A) such that max{f,g}̸∈M (X,A)? Why?

Exam Answer

Let \(\{f_n\}\subset\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A)\). For every \(\alpha\in\mathbb R\), \[ \left\{\sup_n f_n>\alpha\right\} =\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty \{f_n>\alpha\}\in\mathcal A, \] hence \(\sup_n f_n\) is measurable. Also \[ \inf_n f_n=-\sup_n(-f_n), \] so \(\inf_n f_n\) is measurable.

Moreover, \[ \limsup_{n\to\infty}f_n=\inf_{k\ge1}\sup_{n\ge k}f_n, \qquad \liminf_{n\to\infty}f_n=\sup_{k\ge1}\inf_{n\ge k}f_n, \] hence both are measurable.

No: if \(f,g\in\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A)\), then \[ \max\{f,g\}=\sup\{f,g\}, \] so \(\max\{f,g\}\) is always measurable.

Question 1.43. State and prove the vanishing lemma for functionsf∈M +(X,A).

Exam Answer

Let \(f\in\mathcal M^+(X,\mathcal A)\). If \[ \int_X f\,d\mu=0, \] then \(f=0\) a.e. in \(X\).

Proof. It is enough to show \(\mu(\{f>0\})=0\). Since \[ \{f>0\}=\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty \left\{f>\frac1n\right\}, \] it suffices to prove each set has measure zero. By Chebyshev’s inequality, \[ \mu\left(\left\{f>\frac1n\right\}\right) \le n\int_X f\,d\mu=0. \] Thus all these sets have measure zero, and by countable subadditivity, \[ \mu(\{f>0\})=0. \] Hence \(f=0\) a.e.

Question 1.44. State and prove the Monotone Convergence Theorem (or Beppo Levi Theorem).

Exam Answer

Monotone Convergence Theorem (Beppo Levi).
Let \((X,\mathcal{A},\mu)\) be a measure space and let \(\{f_n\}\subset \mathcal{M}_+(X,\mathcal{A})\) satisfy \[ f_n(x)\le f_{n+1}(x)\quad\forall x,\ n,\qquad f_n(x)\to f(x)\ \text{pointwise}. \] Then \(f\in\mathcal{M}_+(X,\mathcal{A})\) and \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_X f_n\,d\mu=\int_X f\,d\mu. \]

Proof.
Let \[ \alpha:=\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_X f_n\,d\mu\in[0,+\infty]. \] Since \(f_n\le f\), monotonicity gives \[ \alpha\le \int_X f\,d\mu. \] For the reverse inequality, fix simple \(s\) with \(0\le s\le f\) and \(\varepsilon\in(0,1)\). Define \[ E_n:=\{x:\ f_n(x)\ge (1-\varepsilon)s(x)\}. \] Then \(E_n\uparrow X\), and \[ \int_X f_n\,d\mu \ge \int_{E_n} f_n\,d\mu \ge (1-\varepsilon)\int_{E_n}s\,d\mu. \] Let \(n\to\infty\) and use continuity from below: \[ \alpha\ge (1-\varepsilon)\int_X s\,d\mu. \] Let \(\varepsilon\downarrow 0\): \[ \alpha\ge \int_X s\,d\mu. \] Taking supremum over simple \(s\le f\) yields \[ \alpha\ge \int_X f\,d\mu. \] Hence equality.

Failure without monotonicity. On \(\mathbb{R}\), \(f_n=\chi_{[n,n+1]}\) satisfies \(f_n\to 0\) pointwise but \[ \int_{\mathbb{R}} f_n\,d\lambda=1 \not\to 0. \]

Question 1.45. State and prove Fatou’s Lemma.

Exam Answer

Fatou’s Lemma.
If \(\{f_n\}\subset\mathcal{M}_+(X,\mathcal{A})\), then \[ \int_X \liminf_{n\to\infty} f_n\,d\mu \le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\int_X f_n\,d\mu. \]

Proof. Define \[ g_k(x):=\inf_{m\ge k} f_m(x). \] Then \(g_k\in\mathcal{M}_+\), \(g_k\le g_{k+1}\), and \[ g_k(x)\uparrow \liminf_{n\to\infty} f_n(x). \] Also, for every \(m\ge k\), \(g_k\le f_m\), so \[ \int_X g_k\,d\mu \le \int_X f_m\,d\mu\quad\forall m\ge k. \] Hence \[ \int_X g_k\,d\mu \le \inf_{m\ge k}\int_X f_m\,d\mu. \] Let \(k\to\infty\). By MCT on \(g_k\): \[ \int_X \liminf_{n\to\infty} f_n\,d\mu = \lim_{k\to\infty}\int_X g_k\,d\mu \le \lim_{k\to\infty}\inf_{m\ge k}\int_X f_m\,d\mu = \liminf_{n\to\infty}\int_X f_n\,d\mu. \]

Question 1.54. State and prove the Lebesgue theorem. In which case it is simple to find a dominating function?

Exam Answer

Lebesgue Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT).
Let \(f_n:X\to\mathbb{R}\) be measurable, \(f_n\to f\) a.e., and assume there exists \(g\in L^1(X)\) such that \[ |f_n(x)|\le g(x)\quad\text{a.e. for all }n. \] Then: 1. \(f\in L^1(X)\); 2. \(f_n\to f\) in \(L^1\), i.e. \[ \int_X |f_n-f|\,d\mu\to 0; \] 3. therefore \[ \int_X f_n\,d\mu \to \int_X f\,d\mu. \]

Proof via Fatou.
From \(|f_n|\le g\) and a.e. convergence, \(|f|\le g\) a.e., so \(f\in L^1\). Also: \[ |f_n-f|\le |f_n|+|f|\le 2g. \] Define \[ h_n:=2g-|f_n-f|\ge 0. \] Fatou gives \[ \int_X \liminf h_n\,d\mu \le \liminf \int_X h_n\,d\mu. \] Since \(|f_n-f|\to 0\) a.e., \(\liminf h_n=2g\) a.e. Hence \[ \int_X 2g\,d\mu \le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\left(\int_X 2g\,d\mu-\int_X |f_n-f|\,d\mu\right) = \int_X 2g\,d\mu-\limsup_{n\to\infty}\int_X |f_n-f|\,d\mu. \] Therefore \[ \limsup_{n\to\infty}\int_X |f_n-f|\,d\mu\le 0. \] Since integrals of absolute values are nonnegative, \(\int_X |f_n-f|\,d\mu\to 0\).

Simple dominator case. If \(\mu(X)<\infty\) and \(|f_n|\le M\) a.e. for all \(n\), then \(g\equiv M\) is in \(L^1(X)\): \[ \int_X g\,d\mu=M\,\mu(X)<\infty. \]

Question 1.58. For a sequence of functions{fn}∈M, write the definitions of: pointwise convergence; uniform convergence; almost everywhere convergence; convergence inL1; convergence inL∞; convergence in measure.

Exam Answer

Let \(\{f_n\}\subseteq \mathcal{M}(X,\mathcal{A})\), \(f\in\mathcal{M}(X,\mathcal{A})\).

Pointwise convergence. \[ f_n\to f \text{ pointwise in }X \iff \forall x\in X,\ \lim_{n\to\infty}f_n(x)=f(x). \]

Uniform convergence. \[ f_n\to f \text{ uniformly in }X \iff \lim_{n\to\infty}\sup_{x\in X}|f_n(x)-f(x)|=0. \]

Almost everywhere convergence. \[ f_n\to f \text{ a.e. in }X \iff \exists N\in\mathcal{A},\ \mu(N)=0,\ \forall x\in X\setminus N:\ f_n(x)\to f(x). \]

Convergence in \(L^1\). \[ f_n\to f \text{ in }L^1 \iff \lim_{n\to\infty}\|f_n-f\|_{L^1} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\int_X |f_n-f|\,d\mu =0. \]

Convergence in \(L^\infty\). \[ f_n\to f \text{ in }L^\infty \iff \lim_{n\to\infty}\|f_n-f\|_{L^\infty} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\operatorname*{ess\,sup}_{x\in X}|f_n(x)-f(x)| =0. \]

Convergence in measure. \[ f_n\to f \text{ in measure on }X \iff \forall \varepsilon>0,\ \mu\bigl(\{|f_n-f|\ge \varepsilon\}\bigr)\xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}0. \]

Useful implications and failures. \[ \text{uniform}\Rightarrow\text{pointwise},\qquad \text{uniform}\Rightarrow L^\infty,\qquad L^1\Rightarrow\text{convergence in measure}. \] Convergence in measure implies a.e. convergence only up to subsequences. On finite measure spaces, a.e. convergence alone does not imply \(L^1\) convergence.

Question 1.59. Is it true that iffn→f in measure, thenfn→f a.e.? Justify the answer.

Exam Answer

Question 1.59. Is it true that if \(f_n \to f\) in measure, then \(f_n \to f\) a.e.? Justify the answer.

Short answer. No.

Reason.
Convergence in measure controls the size of bad sets, not their location.
If bad sets move around, each point can still be hit infinitely many times.

Counterexample (typewriter sequence on \([0,1]\)).
Define a sequence of characteristic functions of dyadic intervals that sweep \([0,1]\): - level \(k=0\): \(\chi_{[0,1]}\); - level \(k=1\): \(\chi_{[0,1/2]},\ \chi_{[1/2,1]}\); - level \(k=2\): \(\chi_{[0,1/4]},\ \chi_{[1/4,1/2]},\ \chi_{[1/2,3/4]},\ \chi_{[3/4,1]}\); - etc.

Each term equals \(1\) on an interval of length \(2^{-k}\) and \(0\) elsewhere.

For every \(\varepsilon\in(0,1)\): \[ \mu\!\left(\{|f_n-0|>\varepsilon\}\right)=\text{length of support}\to 0, \] so \(f_n\to 0\) in measure.

But for every \(x\in[0,1]\), the sequence \(f_n(x)\) takes value \(1\) infinitely many times and \(0\) infinitely many times, hence it does not converge pointwise anywhere. Therefore, not a.e.

Patch theorem to remember:.
If \(f_n\to f\) in measure, there exists a subsequence \(f_{n_k}\to f\) a.e.

Question 1.60. What is the relation between convergence in measure and convergence a.e. up to subsequences?

Exam Answer

Question 1.60. What is the relation between convergence in measure and convergence a.e. up to subsequences?

Theorem (Riesz subsequence theorem):. \[ f_n \to f \text{ in measure} \ \Longrightarrow\ \exists\ \{n_k\}\ \text{such that}\ f_{n_k}\to f \text{ a.e.} \]

Interpretation.
Convergence in measure is noisy global control.
A subsequence can be selected so that bad sets are summable, which forces pointwise stabilization outside a null set.

Why full-sequence implication fails.
Typewriter sequence: converges in measure to \(0\), but not a.e. for the full sequence.

Question 1.61. Under which hypothesis onX, does convergence a.e. imply convergence in measure? What happens if one omits the key assumption onX?

Exam Answer

Question 1.61. Under which hypothesis on \(X\) does convergence a.e. imply convergence in measure? What happens if one omits the key assumption on \(X\)?

Key hypothesis: \(\mu(X)<\infty\) (finite measure space).

Theorem.
If \(\mu(X)<\infty\) and \(f_n\to f\) a.e., then \(f_n\to f\) in measure.

Failure when \(\mu(X)=\infty\).
On \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{L},\lambda)\), let \[ f_n=\chi_{[n,\infty)}. \] For each fixed \(x\), eventually \(n>x\), so \(f_n(x)\to 0\) pointwise everywhere. But for \(\varepsilon=\frac12\): \[ \{|f_n-0|\ge \tfrac12\}=[n,\infty),\qquad \lambda([n,\infty))=\infty, \] so this does not go to \(0\). Hence no convergence in measure.

Question 1.62. Show that convergence inL1 implies convergence in measure.

Exam Answer

Claim.
If \(f_n \to f\) in \(L^1(X,\mathcal{A},\mu)\), then \(f_n \to f\) in measure on \(X\).

Proof (Chebyshev bridge).
Assume \[ \|f_n-f\|_{L^1}=\int_X |f_n-f|\,d\mu \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}0. \] Fix \(\varepsilon>0\) and define \[ E_{n,\varepsilon}:=\{x\in X:\ |f_n(x)-f(x)|\ge \varepsilon\}. \] Apply Chebyshev inequality to \(g_n:=|f_n-f|\): \[ \mu(E_{n,\varepsilon}) = \mu(\{g_n\ge \varepsilon\}) \le \frac1\varepsilon \int_X g_n\,d\mu = \frac1\varepsilon \|f_n-f\|_{L^1}. \] Taking limits: \[ 0\le \limsup_{n\to\infty}\mu(E_{n,\varepsilon}) \le \frac1\varepsilon \lim_{n\to\infty}\|f_n-f\|_{L^1} =0. \] Hence \(\mu(E_{n,\varepsilon})\to 0\) for every \(\varepsilon>0\), i.e. \(f_n\to f\) in measure.

Failure of the converse.
In general, convergence in measure does not imply \(L^1\) convergence. A standard counterexample is \[ f_n=n\,\chi_{[0,1/n]} \quad\text{on }(0,1). \] Then \(f_n\to 0\) in measure, but \[ \|f_n\|_{L^1}=\int_0^1 n\,\chi_{[0,1/n]}(x)\,dx=1, \] so \(f_n \not\to 0\) in \(L^1\).

Question 1.63. Show that convergence inL1 implies convergence a.e. up to subsequences.

Exam Answer

Question 1.63. Show that convergence in \(L^1\) implies convergence a.e. up to subsequences.

Assume \[ \|f_n-f\|_{L^1}\to 0. \]

From Question 1.62, this implies \(f_n\to f\) in measure.

Now choose a subsequence \(\{f_{n_k}\}\) such that for every \(k\): \[ \mu\!\left(\left\{|f_{n_k}-f|\ge 2^{-k}\right\}\right)<2^{-k}. \] Define \[ E_k:=\{|f_{n_k}-f|\ge 2^{-k}\},\qquad A:=\limsup_{k\to\infty}E_k =\bigcap_{j=1}^\infty \bigcup_{k=j}^\infty E_k. \] Then \[ \mu\!\left(\bigcup_{k=j}^\infty E_k\right)\le \sum_{k=j}^\infty \mu(E_k) <\sum_{k=j}^\infty 2^{-k}=2^{-j+1}\to 0, \] hence \(\mu(A)=0\).

If \(x\notin A\), then \(x\in E_k\) only finitely many times, so for large \(k\): \[ |f_{n_k}(x)-f(x)|<2^{-k}\to 0. \] Therefore \[ f_{n_k}(x)\to f(x)\quad\text{for a.e. }x. \]

Question 1.64. Does convergence in measure imply convergence inL1? Does convergence a.e. imply convergence inL1? Justify the answer.

Exam Answer

Answer: no to both in general.

  1. Convergence in measure \(\not\Rightarrow L^1\).
    Use \[ f_n(x)=n\,\chi_{[0,1/n]}(x)\quad\text{on }(0,1). \] For each \(\varepsilon>0\), for \(n>\varepsilon\): \[ \{|f_n|>\varepsilon\}=[0,1/n],\qquad \mu(\{|f_n|>\varepsilon\})=\frac1n\to 0, \] so \(f_n\to 0\) in measure. But \[ \|f_n\|_{L^1}=1 \quad \forall n, \] hence no \(L^1\) convergence.

  2. a.e. convergence \(\not\Rightarrow L^1\).
    The same spike sequence gives a.e. convergence to \(0\): for every \(x>0\), eventually \(x>1/n\), so \(f_n(x)=0\).
    Yet \(\|f_n\|_{L^1}=1\) for all \(n\), so no \(L^1\) convergence.

Alternative a.e. counterexample.
On \(\mathbb{R}\): \[ f_n=\chi_{[n,n+1]}. \] Then \(f_n(x)\to 0\) for every fixed \(x\), but \[ \|f_n\|_{L^1(\mathbb{R})}=1 \quad \forall n. \]

Recovery of \(L^1\) convergence.
Add domination: if \(f_n\to f\) a.e. and \(|f_n|\le g\) a.e. with \(g\in L^1\), then by DCT: \[ \|f_n-f\|_{L^1}\to 0. \]

Exam Answer

Question 1.65. Does convergence in \(L^1\) (or in measure) imply a.e. convergence? Justify the answer.

Short answer: No, in general.

Reason:
\(L^1\) and measure convergence control size of error sets, but a.e. convergence requires pointwise stabilization at fixed points. A moving error pattern can defeat pointwise convergence.

Counterexample (typewriter on \([0,1]\)):
Let \(f_n\) be the dyadic sweeping characteristic-function sequence described in Question 1.59. Then: 1. \(f_n\to 0\) in measure; 2. in fact, \(\|f_n\|_{L^1}=\text{length of support}\to 0\), so \(f_n\to 0\) in \(L^1\); 3. \(f_n(x)\) does not converge for any \(x\in[0,1]\).

Hence neither convergence in measure nor convergence in \(L^1\) implies a.e. convergence for the whole sequence.

Subsequence correction:
Both from measure convergence and from \(L^1\) convergence, one can always extract a subsequence converging a.e.

Question 1.66. Write the definition of Lebesgue point. What is about the measure of the set of points that are not Lebesgue points for a functionf∈L1?

Exam Answer

Let \(f\in L^1(a,b)\). A point \(x_0\in(a,b)\) is a Lebesgue point of \(f\) if \[ \lim_{h\to0}\frac1{|h|}\int_{x_0}^{x_0+h}|f(t)-f(x_0)|\,dt=0. \] Equivalently, the average oscillation of \(f\) around \(x_0\) tends to zero.

The Lebesgue differentiation theorem says that, if \(f\in L^1(a,b)\), then a.e. \(x_0\in(a,b)\) is a Lebesgue point of \(f\). Therefore \[ \lambda\big(\{x\in(a,b):x\text{ is not a Lebesgue point of }f\}\big)=0. \]

Question 1.67. State and prove the First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus forf∈L1.

Exam Answer

Let \(f\in L^1(a,b)\) and define \[ F(x):=\int_a^x f(t)\,dt. \] Then \(F\) is differentiable a.e. in \((a,b)\) and \[ F'(x)=f(x)\qquad\text{for a.e. }x\in(a,b). \]

Proof. Let \(x\) be a Lebesgue point of \(f\). For \(h\ne0\) with \(x+h\in(a,b)\), \[ \frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h}-f(x) =\frac1h\int_x^{x+h}(f(t)-f(x))\,dt. \] Hence \[ \left|\frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h}-f(x)\right| \le \frac1{|h|}\int_x^{x+h}|f(t)-f(x)|\,dt\to0. \] Thus \(F'(x)=f(x)\) at every Lebesgue point. Since a.e. point is a Lebesgue point, the conclusion follows.

Question 1.3. Write the definition of the Borelσ−algebra in a metric space. Provide classes of Borel sets. CharacterizeB(R),B(R) andB(RN ).

Exam Answer

Let \((X,d)\) be a metric space and let \(\tau_d\) be the family of open subsets of \(X\). The Borel sigma-algebra is \[ \mathcal B(X):=\sigma(\tau_d), \] the smallest sigma-algebra containing all open sets of \(X\). Its elements are called Borel sets.

In particular, the following are Borel sets: open sets, closed sets, countable unions of closed sets \((F_\sigma)\), and countable intersections of open sets \((G_\delta)\).

For \(\mathbb R\), \[ \mathcal B(\mathbb R)=\sigma\{(a,b):a<b\} =\sigma\{[a,b]:a\le b\} =\sigma\{(a,b]:a\le b\}. \] Equivalently, it is generated by open rays or closed rays.

For the extended real line \(\overline{\mathbb R}=\mathbb R\cup\{-\infty,+\infty\}\), \[ \mathcal B(\overline{\mathbb R}) =\sigma\{(a,+\infty]:a\in\mathbb R\}. \]

For \(\mathbb R^N\), \[ \mathcal B(\mathbb R^N) =\sigma\{\text{open rectangles}\} =\sigma\{\text{closed rectangles}\}. \]

Question 1.22. Write the definitions of: a) Borel measurable functions; b) Lebesgue measurable functions.

Exam Answer

Let \((X,d)\) and \((Y,\rho)\) be metric spaces, with Borel sigma-algebras \(\mathcal B(X)\) and \(\mathcal B(Y)\). Let \(\mathcal L(X)\) denote the Lebesgue sigma-algebra when \(X\subseteq\mathbb R^N\).

A function \(f:X\to Y\) is Borel measurable if \[ f^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal B(X)\qquad\forall E\in\mathcal B(Y). \] Equivalently, \(f:(X,\mathcal B(X))\to(Y,\mathcal B(Y))\) is measurable.

A function \(f:X\to Y\) is Lebesgue measurable if \[ f^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal L(X)\qquad\forall E\in\mathcal B(Y). \] Equivalently, \(f:(X,\mathcal L(X))\to(Y,\mathcal B(Y))\) is measurable.

Question 1.23. Prove that: (i) B−measurable functions are alsoL-measurable; (ii) continuous functions are both Borel and Lebesgue measurable.

Exam Answer

  1. Let \(f:X\to Y\) be Borel measurable. Then for every \(E\in\mathcal B(Y)\), \[ f^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal B(X). \] Since \(\mathcal B(X)\subseteq\mathcal L(X)\), we also have \(f^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal L(X)\). Hence \(f\) is Lebesgue measurable.

  2. Let \(f:X\to Y\) be continuous. For every open set \(O\subseteq Y\), \(f^{-1}(O)\) is open in \(X\), hence Borel. Since \(\mathcal B(Y)\) is generated by open sets, the standard generator criterion gives \[ f^{-1}(E)\in\mathcal B(X)\qquad\forall E\in\mathcal B(Y). \] Thus \(f\) is Borel measurable. By part (i), \(f\) is also Lebesgue measurable.

Question 1.27. Let f,g ∈ M(X,A). What can we say about measurability of{f < g},{f ≤ g},{f =g}?

Exam Answer

Let \(f,g\in\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A)\). Then \[ \{f<g\},\qquad \{f\le g\},\qquad \{f=g\} \] belong to \(\mathcal A\).

Indeed, \[ \{f<g\}=\bigcup_{q\in\mathbb Q}\big(\{f<q\}\cap\{q<g\}\big), \] which is measurable because it is a countable union of measurable sets. Then \[ \{f\le g\}=\{g<f\}^c, \qquad \{f=g\}=\{f\le g\}\cap\{g\le f\}. \]

Question 1.29. Let f,g ∈M (X,A). Is it true thatf +g,fg ∈ (X,A)?

Exam Answer

If \(f,g:X\to\mathbb R\) are measurable, then \[ f+g\in\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A), \qquad fg\in\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A). \]

Indeed, the map \(x\mapsto (f(x),g(x))\) from \(X\) to \(\mathbb R^2\) is measurable, and the maps \[ S(u,v)=u+v, \qquad P(u,v)=uv \] are continuous on \(\mathbb R^2\). Therefore \(f+g=S\circ(f,g)\) and \(fg=P\circ(f,g)\) are measurable.

For extended real-valued functions, one must exclude undefined forms such as \(+\infty-\infty\).

Question 1.32. Prove thatA is measurable if and only ifχA is a measurable function.

Question 1.33. Prove or disprove the following statements: (a) f∈M (X,A)⇔f±∈M +(X,A); (b) f∈M (X,A)⇔|f|∈M (X,A).

Exam Answer

  1. The statement is true: \[ f\in\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A) \iff f^+,f^-\in\mathcal M^+(X,\mathcal A). \] If \(f\) is measurable, then \[ f^+=\max\{f,0\},\qquad f^-=\max\{-f,0\} \] are measurable and nonnegative. Conversely, if \(f^+\) and \(f^-\) are measurable, then \[ f=f^+-f^- \] is measurable, provided the difference is well-defined.

  2. The statement is false. The implication \[ f\text{ measurable}\Rightarrow |f|\text{ measurable} \] holds because \(|f|=f^++f^-\). Question 1.34. Write the definition of simple function. What is its canonical form? How can we characterize measurability of a simple function? Write the definition of step function.

Exam Answer

A function \(s:X\to\mathbb R\) is simple if \(s(X)\) is finite. If \[ s(X)=\{c_1,\dots,c_m\},\qquad c_i\ne c_j\ (i\ne j), \] and \[ E_k:=\{x\in X:s(x)=c_k\}, \] then \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^m\) is a partition of \(X\) and \[ s=\sum_{k=1}^m c_k\chi_{E_k} \] is the canonical form.

If \((X,\mathcal A)\) is measurable, then \(s\) is measurable iff \[ E_k\in\mathcal A\qquad k=1,\dots,m. \]

A step function on \(\mathbb R\) is a simple function whose level sets are intervals, or finite unions of intervals, depending on the convention used in the course.

Question 1.35. State and give a sketch of the proof of the Simple Approximation Theorem.

Exam Answer

Let \((X,\mathcal A)\) be a measurable space and let \(f:X\to\overline{\mathbb R}\) be measurable. Then there exists a sequence of measurable simple functions \(s_n\) such that \[ s_n(x)\to f(x)\qquad\forall x\in X. \] If \(f\ge0\), one can choose \[ 0\le s_n\le s_{n+1}\le f, \qquad s_n\uparrow f. \] If \(f\) is bounded, the convergence can be chosen uniform.

Sketch for \(0\le f\le1\). For \(k=0,\dots,2^n-1\), set \[ E_{k,n}:=\left\{x:\frac{k}{2^n}\le f(x)<\frac{k+1}{2^n}\right\} \] and define \[ s_n:=\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1}\frac{k}{2^n}\chi_{E_{k,n}}. \] The sets \(E_{k,n}\) are measurable, hence \(s_n\) is simple and measurable. Also \[ 0\le f-s_n\le2^{-n}, \] so \(s_n\to f\) uniformly. General nonnegative \(f\) follows by truncation and the same dyadic construction; general \(f\) follows by applying the result to \(f^+\) and \(f^-\).

Question 1.56. State the theorem for integration of series (without sign restriction on the general term fn).

Exam Answer

Let \(f_n\in L^1(X,\mathcal A,\mu)\) and assume \[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \int_X |f_n|\,d\mu<\infty. \] Then the series \[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n(x) \] converges absolutely for a.e. \(x\in X\), its sum belongs to \(L^1\), and \[ \int_X \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n\,d\mu = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \int_X f_n\,d\mu. \]

Indeed, by the nonnegative series theorem, \[ \int_X \sum_{n=1}^\infty |f_n|\,d\mu = \sum_{n=1}^\infty\int_X |f_n|\,d\mu<\infty, \] so \(\sum |f_n|<\infty\) a.e. Then the partial sums are dominated by \(\sum |f_n|\in L^1\), and dominated convergence gives the exchange of sum and integral.

Question 1.68. State the Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Exam Answer

For \(\varphi:[a,b]\to\mathbb R\), the following are equivalent:

  1. \(\varphi\in AC([a,b])\).
  2. \(\varphi\) is differentiable a.e., \(\varphi'\in L^1(a,b)\), and \[ \varphi(x)-\varphi(a)=\int_a^x \varphi'(t)\,dt \qquad\forall x\in[a,b]. \]

Thus absolute continuity is exactly the condition under which the classical integral recovery formula holds in the Lebesgue sense.

Question 1.69. State and prove the characterization of absolutely continuous functions in terms of the integration by parts formula.

Exam Answer

Characterization theorem (Sobolev form).
For \(I=[a,b]\) and \(u\in L^1(I)\), the following are equivalent: 1. \(u\in AC(I)\). 2. There exists \(v\in L^1(I)\) such that for every \(\varphi\in C_c^\infty((a,b))\), \[ \int_a^b u(x)\,\varphi'(x)\,dx = -\int_a^b v(x)\,\varphi(x)\,dx. \] Equivalently, \(u\in W^{1,1}(I)\) and its weak derivative is \(u'=v\).

Proof of \((1)\Rightarrow(2)\).
If \(u\in AC([a,b])\), then \(u\) is a.e. differentiable, \(u'\in L^1(a,b)\), and \[ u(x)=u(a)+\int_a^x u'(t)\,dt. \] Set \(v:=u'\). For \(\varphi\in C_c^\infty((a,b))\), classical integration by parts gives \[ \int_a^b u\varphi' = [u\varphi]_a^b-\int_a^b u'\varphi = -\int_a^b v\varphi \] because \(\varphi(a)=\varphi(b)=0\).

Proof of \((2)\Rightarrow(1)\).
Assume there exists \(v\in L^1(I)\) with \[ \int_a^b u\varphi'=-\int_a^b v\varphi \quad\forall \varphi\in C_c^\infty((a,b)). \] Define \[ z(x):=\int_a^x v(t)\,dt. \] Then \(z\in AC([a,b])\) and \(z'=v\) a.e., so \(z\) has weak derivative \(v\).
Let \(h:=u-z\). Its weak derivative is \(0\), i.e. \[ \int_a^b h\,\varphi'=0 \quad\forall \varphi\in C_c^\infty((a,b)). \] By the vanishing-derivative lemma, \(h\) is a.e. constant: \(h=C\) a.e. Hence \[ u(x)=z(x)+C=\int_a^x v(t)\,dt+C \quad\text{a.e.} \] The right-hand side is absolutely continuous; replacing \(u\) by its a.e.-equal representative gives \(u\in AC([a,b])\).

Question 1.70. Write the definitions of weak derivative and ofW 1,p.

Exam Answer

Let \(I=(a,b)\). Given \(u,v\in L^1_{loc}(I)\), we say that \(v\) is the weak derivative of \(u\) if \[ \int_I u\varphi'\,dx=-\int_I v\varphi\,dx \qquad\forall\varphi\in C_c^\infty(I). \] In that case we write \(u'=v\) weakly. The weak derivative is unique up to equality a.e.

For \(1\le p\le\infty\), \[ W^{1,p}(I):=\{u\in L^p(I):\text{ the weak derivative }u'\text{ exists and }u'\in L^p(I)\}. \] For \(1\le p<\infty\), \[ \|u\|_{W^{1,p}}:=\left(\int_I |u|^p+\int_I |u'|^p\right)^{1/p}. \] For \(p=\infty\), \[ \|u\|_{W^{1,\infty}}:=\|u\|_\infty+\|u'\|_\infty. \]

Question 1.71. What is the relation betweenW 1,1 and AC? Justify the answer.

Exam Answer

On a bounded interval \(I=[a,b]\), \[ W^{1,1}(I)=AC(I) \] up to equality a.e.

If \(u\in AC(I)\), then \(u'\) exists a.e., \(u'\in L^1(I)\), and \[ u(x)=u(a)+\int_a^x u'(t)\,dt. \] Integration by parts against \(\varphi\in C_c^\infty(I)\) shows that the classical derivative is also the weak derivative, hence \(u\in W^{1,1}(I)\).

Conversely, if \(u\in W^{1,1}(I)\) with weak derivative \(v\in L^1(I)\), define \[ z(x):=\int_a^x v(t)\,dt. \] Then \(z\in AC(I)\) and \(z'=v\) a.e. The function \(u-z\) has weak derivative zero, hence is a.e. constant. Thus \(u=z+C\) a.e.; choosing this absolutely continuous representative gives \(u\in AC(I)\).

Question 1.18. Write the excision property and prove it. Write and prove (partially) the theorem concerning the regularity of the Lebesgue measure onR.

Exam Answer

.Excision Property (clean statement)**
Let \(A \in \mathscr{L}(\mathbb{R})\) with \(\lambda^*(A) < \infty\). If \(A \subseteq B\) (with \(B \subseteq \mathbb{R}\), not necessarily measurable), then \[ \lambda^*(B \setminus A) = \lambda^*(B) - \lambda^*(A). \]

Proof (from Caratheodory condition).
Since \(A\) is measurable, for every \(Z \subseteq \mathbb{R}\), \[ \lambda^*(Z)=\lambda^*(Z\cap A)+\lambda^*(Z\cap A^c). \] Choose \(Z=B\). Because \(A\subseteq B\), we have \(B\cap A=A\), and \(B\cap A^c=B\setminus A\). Hence \[ \lambda^*(B)=\lambda^*(A)+\lambda^*(B\setminus A). \] Since \(\lambda^*(A)<\infty\), subtraction is legitimate: \[ \lambda^*(B\setminus A)=\lambda^*(B)-\lambda^*(A). \]

Regularity of Lebesgue measure (equivalent forms)
For \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\), the following are equivalent: 1. \(E \in \mathscr{L}(\mathbb{R})\). 2. For every \(\varepsilon>0\), there exists open \(O \supseteq E\) such that \(\lambda^*(O\setminus E)<\varepsilon\). 3. There exists \(G \in G_\delta\) with \(E\subseteq G\) and \(\lambda^*(G\setminus E)=0\). 4. For every \(\varepsilon>0\), there exists closed \(C \subseteq E\) such that \(\lambda^*(E\setminus C)<\varepsilon\). 5. There exists \(F \in F_\sigma\) with \(F\subseteq E\) and \(\lambda^*(E\setminus F)=0\).

Partial proof sketch \((i)\Rightarrow(ii)\Rightarrow(iii)\)
Assume \(E \in \mathscr{L}(\mathbb{R})\), \(\lambda(E)<\infty\), and fix \(\varepsilon>0\). - By definition of outer measure, choose open intervals \(\{I_k\}\) covering \(E\) such that \[ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\lambda(I_k)<\lambda^*(E)+\varepsilon. \] - Let \(O=\bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty}I_k\). Then \(O\) is open, \(E\subseteq O\), and \[ \lambda^*(O)\le \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\lambda(I_k)<\lambda^*(E)+\varepsilon. \] - By excision (measurable finite \(E\)): \[ \lambda^*(O\setminus E)=\lambda^*(O)-\lambda^*(E)<\varepsilon. \] This proves (ii). - For each \(k\in\mathbb{N}\), apply (ii) with \(\varepsilon=1/k\): there exists open \(O_k\supseteq E\) with \[ \lambda^*(O_k\setminus E)<\frac1k. \] - Define \[ G:=\bigcap_{k=1}^{\infty}O_k. \] Then \(G\in G_\delta\), \(E\subseteq G\), and \(G\setminus E\subseteq O_k\setminus E\) for every \(k\). Hence \[ \lambda^*(G\setminus E)\le \lambda^*(O_k\setminus E)<\frac1k \quad \forall k, \] so \(\lambda^*(G\setminus E)=0\). Thus (iii).

Question 1.38. Write the definitions of the Lebesgue integral of a nonnegative measurable simple function overX and over a measurable subsetE⊆X. Write the main properties of the integral and prove some of them.

Question 1.40. Write the two possible equivalent definitions of Lebesgue integral of a measurable nonnegative function.

For a measurable \(f:X\to\overline{\mathbb R}\), \[ f^+=\max\{f,0\},\qquad f^-=\max\{-f,0\}, \qquad f=f^+-f^-,\quad |f|=f^++f^-. \] Then \[ f\in L^1 \iff \int_X f^+\,d\mu<\infty\text{ and }\int_X f^-\,d\mu<\infty \iff \int_X |f|\,d\mu<\infty. \] Indeed, \[ \int_X|f|\,d\mu=\int_Xf^+\,d\mu+\int_Xf^-\,d\mu. \]

If \(f\in L^1\), then \[ \left|\int_Xf\,d\mu\right| = \left|\int_Xf^+\,d\mu-\int_Xf^-\,d\mu\right| \le \int_Xf^+\,d\mu+\int_Xf^-\,d\mu = \int_X|f|\,d\mu. \]

Equivalently, \[ L^1(X,\mathcal A,\mu) = \left\{f\in\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A):\int_X|f|\,d\mu<\infty\right\}/\sim, \] where \(f\sim g\) iff \(f=g\) a.e.

Question 2.12. State and prove the Riesz Theorem.

Exam Answer

Let \(X\) be a normed space. If \(\dim X=\infty\), then the closed unit ball \[ \overline B_1(0):=\{x\in X:\|x\|\le1\} \] is not compact. Equivalently, if \(\overline B_1(0)\) is compact, then \(X\) is finite-dimensional.

Proof. Assume \(\dim X=\infty\). Choose \(x_1\in X\) with \(\|x_1\|=1\) and set \[ Y_1:=\operatorname{span}\{x_1\}. \] Then \(Y_1\) is finite-dimensional, hence closed, and \(Y_1\ne X\). By Riesz lemma with \(\varepsilon=1/2\), choose \(x_2\) with \(\|x_2\|=1\) and \[ \operatorname{dist}(x_2,Y_1)\ge\frac12. \] Inductively, after choosing \(x_1,\dots,x_n\), set \[ Y_n:=\operatorname{span}\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}. \] Again \(Y_n\) is closed and proper, so Riesz lemma gives \(x_{n+1}\) with \(\|x_{n+1}\|=1\) and \[ \operatorname{dist}(x_{n+1},Y_n)\ge\frac12. \] Thus, for \(i<j\), \[ \|x_j-x_i\|\ge \operatorname{dist}(x_j,Y_{j-1})\ge\frac12. \] So \(\{x_n\}\subset\overline B_1(0)\) has no Cauchy subsequence, hence no convergent subsequence. Since metric compactness implies sequential compactness, \(\overline B_1(0)\) is not compact.

Question 2.17. Write and show the Holder inequality.

Exam Answer

Let \(p,q\in[1,\infty]\) be conjugate. If \(f\in L^p\) and \(g\in L^q\), then \(fg\in L^1\) and \[ \|fg\|_1\le \|f\|_p\|g\|_q. \]

Proof for \(1<p,q<\infty\). If one norm is zero or infinite, the result is immediate. Otherwise apply Young inequality pointwise to \[ a=\frac{|f|}{\|f\|_p}, \qquad b=\frac{|g|}{\|g\|_q}. \] Then \[ \frac{|fg|}{\|f\|_p\|g\|_q} \le \frac1p\frac{|f|^p}{\|f\|_p^p} + \frac1q\frac{|g|^q}{\|g\|_q^q}. \] Integrating gives \[ \frac{\|fg\|_1}{\|f\|_p\|g\|_q}\le \frac1p+\frac1q=1. \]

If \(p=1\), \(q=\infty\), then \(|g|\le\|g\|_\infty\) a.e., so \[ \|fg\|_1\le \int_X |f|\|g\|_\infty\,d\mu=\|f\|_1\|g\|_\infty. \] The case \(p=\infty\), \(q=1\) is symmetric.

Question 2.22. Show the completeness ofLp spaces.

Exam Answer

For \(1\le p\le\infty\), \(L^p(X,\mathcal A,\mu)\) is complete; hence it is a Banach space.

Proof for \(1\le p<\infty\). Use the series criterion for completeness. Let \(\sum_n\|f_n\|_p<\infty\). Define \[ g_k:=\sum_{n=1}^k |f_n|. \] By Minkowski, \[ \|g_k\|_p\le \sum_{n=1}^k\|f_n\|_p\le M<\infty. \] Since \(g_k\uparrow g:=\sum_n |f_n|\), monotone convergence gives \[ \int g^p\,d\mu=\lim_k\int g_k^p\,d\mu\le M^p. \] Thus \(g\in L^p\), so \(\sum_n f_n\) converges absolutely a.e. Let \[ s_k:=\sum_{n=1}^k f_n, \qquad s:=\sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n. \] Then \(s_k\to s\) a.e. and \[ |s_k-s|^p\le g^p\in L^1. \] By dominated convergence, \[ \|s_k-s\|_p^p=\int |s_k-s|^p\,d\mu\to0. \] Hence every absolutely convergent series converges in \(L^p\), so \(L^p\) is complete. The case \(p=\infty\) follows similarly from uniform essential bounds, or directly from Cauchy convergence in essential sup norm.

Question 2.53. Write the definition of weak convergence. How can it be formulated inLp and in ℓp?

Exam Answer

Definition (weak convergence in normed spaces).
Let \(X\) be a normed space. A sequence \(\{x_n\}\subset X\) converges weakly to \(x\in X\) (notation \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\)) if \[ L(x_n)\to L(x)\qquad \forall L\in X^*. \]

Formulation in \(L^p\) (\(1\le p<\infty\)).
Let \(p'\) be the conjugate exponent, \(\frac1p+\frac1{p'}=1\).
For \(f_n,f\in L^p\): \[ f_n\rightharpoonup f\ \text{in }L^p \iff \int_X f_n g\,d\mu \to \int_X f g\,d\mu \quad \forall g\in L^{p'}. \] (For \(p=\infty\), the dual is larger than \(L^1\); the integral criterion with \(L^1\) is weak-* type.)

Formulation in \(\ell^p\) (\(1\le p<\infty\)).
Let \(x_n=(x_n^{(k)})_{k\ge1}\), \(x=(x^{(k)})_{k\ge1}\) in \(\ell^p\). Then \[ x_n\rightharpoonup x\ \text{in }\ell^p \iff \sum_{k=1}^\infty x_n^{(k)}y^{(k)} \to \sum_{k=1}^\infty x^{(k)}y^{(k)} \quad \forall y=(y^{(k)})\in \ell^{p'}. \]

Useful implication: Strong convergence implies weak convergence.
In general, weak convergence does not imply strong convergence (e.g. standard basis in \(\ell^2\)).

Question 2.54. Show that strong convergence implies weak convergence. Provide a counterexample for the converse implication.

Exam Answer

Theorem. If \(x_n \to x\) strongly in a normed space \(X\), then \(x_n \rightharpoonup x\) weakly.

Proof.
Let \(L\in X^*\). Since \(L\) is continuous (bounded), \[ |L(x_n)-L(x)|=|L(x_n-x)|\le \|L\|\,\|x_n-x\|\xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}0. \] Hence \(L(x_n)\to L(x)\) for every \(L\in X^*\), i.e. \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\).

Counterexample to weak \(\not\Rightarrow\) strong.
In \(\ell^2\), let \(e_n=(0,\dots,0,1,0,\dots)\) be the canonical basis. - For any \(y=(y^{(k)})\in\ell^2\), \[ \langle e_n,y\rangle=y^{(n)}\to 0, \] so \(e_n\rightharpoonup 0\). - But \[ \|e_n\|_{\ell^2}=1 \quad \forall n, \] so \(e_n\not\to 0\) strongly.

Question 2.55. Show that the weak limit is unique.

Exam Answer

Theorem. If \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\) and \(x_n\rightharpoonup y\) in a normed space \(X\), then \(x=y\).

Proof.
For every \(L\in X^*\), \[ L(x_n)\to L(x),\qquad L(x_n)\to L(y), \] thus \(L(x)=L(y)\), i.e. \[ L(x-y)=0 \quad \forall L\in X^*. \] If \(x\ne y\), then \(z:=x-y\ne 0\). By Hahn-Banach (separation corollary), there exists \(L_0\in X^*\) such that \[ L_0(z)\ne 0, \] contradicting \(L(z)=0\) for all \(L\). Therefore \(x=y\).

Question 2.56. If{xn} weakly converges tox, can{xn} be unbounded? State and prove lower semicontinuity w.r.t. weak convergence ofx↦→∥x∥.

Exam Answer

Weakly convergent sequences are bounded.
No. If \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\) in \(X\), then \(\{x_n\}\) is bounded in norm.

Proof.
For each fixed \(L\in X^*\), \(\{L(x_n)\}\) converges in \(\mathbb{R}\), hence is bounded.
So the family \(\{J(x_n)\}\subset X^{**}\) (canonical embedding \(J(x_n)(L)=L(x_n)\)) is pointwise bounded on \(X^*\). By Uniform Boundedness, \[ \sup_n \|J(x_n)\|_{X^{**}}<\infty. \] Since \(\|J(x_n)\|=\|x_n\|\), we get \(\sup_n\|x_n\|<\infty\).

Lower semicontinuity of the norm.
If \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\), then \[ \|x\|\le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\|x_n\|. \]

Proof.
If \(x=0\), trivial. Suppose \(x\neq 0\). By Hahn-Banach, there exists \(L\in X^*\) such that \[ \|L\|=1,\qquad L(x)=\|x\|. \] Since \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\), \[ L(x_n)\to L(x)=\|x\|. \] Hence \[ \|x\|=\lim_{n\to\infty}|L(x_n)|=\liminf_{n\to\infty}|L(x_n)|. \] Also, for each \(n\), \[ |L(x_n)|\le \|L\|\,\|x_n\|=\|x_n\|. \] Taking liminf: \[ \|x\|\le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\|x_n\|. \]

Question 2.57. Show that ifxn weakly converges tox andLn converges toL inX∗, thenLn(xn)→ L(x) as n→∞.

Exam Answer

Let \(X\) be a normed space. If \[ x_n\rightharpoonup x \quad\text{in }X, \qquad L_n\to L \quad\text{strongly in }X^*, \] then \[ L_n(x_n)\to L(x). \]

Proof. Write \[ |L_n(x_n)-L(x)| \le |(L_n-L)(x_n)|+|L(x_n)-L(x)|. \] The second term tends to \(0\) by weak convergence of \(x_n\). For the first term, \[ |(L_n-L)(x_n)|\le \|L_n-L\|_{X^*}\|x_n\|_X. \] Since weakly convergent sequences are bounded, \(\sup_n\|x_n\|<\infty\), and since \(\|L_n-L\|_{X^*}\to0\), the first term tends to \(0\). Hence \(L_n(x_n)\to L(x)\).

Question 2.58. Show thatT∈L (X,Y ) is weak-weak continuous.

Exam Answer

Let \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\). If \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\) in \(X\), then \[ Tx_n\rightharpoonup Tx \quad\text{in }Y. \]

Proof. Take any \(L\in Y^*\). Then \[ L\circ T:X\to\mathbb R \] is linear and continuous, hence \(L\circ T\in X^*\). Since \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\), \[ (L\circ T)(x_n)\to (L\circ T)(x), \] that is, \[ L(Tx_n)\to L(Tx). \] This holds for every \(L\in Y^*\), so \(Tx_n\rightharpoonup Tx\).

Question 2.62. State the Banach-Alaoglu theorem. Why can we say that from a bounded sequence in L∞ we can extract a subsequence which weakly∗ converges inL∞?

Exam Answer

Banach-Alaoglu theorem (standard form).
For any normed space \(X\), the closed unit ball \[ B_{X^*}:=\{L\in X^*:\ \|L\|_{X^*}\le 1\} \] is compact in the weak* topology \(\sigma(X^*,X)\).

Sequential consequence (separable predual).
If \(X\) is separable and \(\{L_n\}\subset X^*\) is bounded, then there exist a subsequence \(L_{n_k}\) and \(L\in X^*\) such that \[ L_{n_k}\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup} L. \]

Application to weak* subsequences in \(L^\infty\).
Using \[ L^\infty(\Omega)\cong (L^1(\Omega))^*, \] and separability of \(L^1(\Omega)\), every bounded sequence in \(L^\infty\) admits a weak* convergent subsequence: \[ f_{n_k}\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup} f \quad\text{in }L^\infty. \] Equivalently, \[ \int_\Omega f_{n_k}g\,d\mu \to \int_\Omega fg\,d\mu \qquad \forall g\in L^1(\Omega). \]

Question 2.63. State and prove the corollary of the Banach-Alaoglu theorem in a separable and reflexive Banach space.

Exam Answer

Let \(X\) be a separable and reflexive Banach space. Every bounded sequence \(\{x_n\}\subset X\) admits a weakly convergent subsequence.

Proof. Since \(X\) is separable and reflexive, \(X^*\) is separable. Let \[ \tau:X\to X^{**} \] be the canonical embedding. If \(\{x_n\}\) is bounded in \(X\), then \(\{\tau(x_n)\}\) is bounded in \(X^{**}=(X^*)^*\).

Apply Banach-Alaoglu in the dual of the separable space \(X^*\): there exist a subsequence \(\tau(x_{n_k})\) and \(\Phi\in X^{**}\) such that \[ \tau(x_{n_k})\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}\Phi \quad\text{in }X^{**}. \] Since \(X\) is reflexive, \(\Phi=\tau(x)\) for some \(x\in X\). Therefore, for every \(L\in X^*\), \[ L(x_{n_k}) =\tau(x_{n_k})(L) \to \tau(x)(L) =L(x). \] Thus \(x_{n_k}\rightharpoonup x\) in \(X\).

Question 2.64. Write the definition of compact operator. Write the definition of operator of finite rank.

Exam Answer

Compact operator.
Let \(X,Y\) be Banach spaces. A linear operator \(K:X\to Y\) is compact if for every bounded set \(E\subset X\), the image \(K(E)\) is relatively compact in \(Y\) (equivalently, \(\overline{K(E)}\) is compact).

Sequential characterization.
\(K\) is compact iff for every bounded sequence \(\{x_n\}\subset X\), the sequence \(\{Kx_n\}\) admits a strongly convergent subsequence in \(Y\).

Finite-rank operator.
An operator \(T\in\mathcal{L}(X,Y)\) has finite rank if \[ \dim(\operatorname{Im}T)<\infty. \]

Question 2.65. How is a compact operator related to operators of finite rank? Can a compact operator be surjective? Justify your answer.

Exam Answer

Finite rank implies compact.
If \(\dim(\operatorname{Im}T)<\infty\), then \(T(B)\) (image of a bounded set) is bounded in a finite-dimensional space. In finite dimension, closed and bounded sets are compact, so \(T\) is compact.

Norm limits of finite-rank operators are compact.
If \(K_n\) are finite-rank and \(K_n\to K\) in operator norm, then \(K\) is compact.

Surjectivity: If \(\dim Y<\infty\), compact surjective operators may exist. If \(\dim Y=\infty\), no compact operator \(K:X\to Y\) can be surjective.

Proof for \(\dim Y=\infty\). Assume \(K:X\to Y\) compact and surjective, with \(X,Y\) Banach and \(\dim Y=\infty\). By the Open Mapping Theorem, \(K(B_X(0,1))\) contains a ball \(B_Y(0,\delta)\) for some \(\delta>0\). Since \(K\) is compact, \(\overline{K(B_X(0,1))}\) is compact, hence \(\overline{B_Y(0,\delta)}\) is compact. But in an infinite-dimensional Banach space, closed balls are not compact (Riesz theorem). Contradiction. Therefore a compact operator onto an infinite-dimensional Banach space cannot be surjective.

Question 2.66. State and prove the theorem about the characterization of compact operators in terms of weak-strong continuity.

Exam Answer

Theorem (compact \(\Rightarrow\) weak-strong continuity). Let \(X,Y\) be Banach spaces and \(T\in\mathcal{K}(X,Y)\). If \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\) in \(X\), then \[ Tx_n\to Tx \quad\text{strongly in }Y. \]

Proof.
Since \(x_n\rightharpoonup x\), the sequence \(\{x_n\}\) is bounded. Compactness of \(T\) implies \(\{Tx_n\}\) is relatively compact, so every subsequence of \(\{Tx_n\}\) has a further strongly convergent subsequence. Take any subsequence \(Tx_{n_k}\). Extract \(Tx_{n_{k_j}}\to y\) strongly. Also \(x_{n_{k_j}}\rightharpoonup x\), and bounded linear maps are weak-weak continuous, so \[ Tx_{n_{k_j}}\rightharpoonup Tx. \] Strong convergence implies weak convergence, hence \(y=Tx\). Therefore every subsequence has a further subsequence converging strongly to \(Tx\), which forces the whole sequence \(Tx_n\to Tx\) strongly.

Converse. If \(X\) is reflexive (in lecture notes often with separability assumptions for sequence extraction), and \(T\in\mathcal{L}(X,Y)\) satisfies \[ x_n\rightharpoonup x \ \Longrightarrow\ Tx_n\to Tx \text{ strongly}, \] then \(T\) is compact.

Proof of the converse. Given bounded \(\{x_n\}\subset X\), reflexivity gives a weakly convergent subsequence \(x_{n_k}\rightharpoonup x\). By the weak-strong property, \(Tx_{n_k}\to Tx\) strongly. Hence every bounded sequence has an image subsequence converging strongly, so \(T\) is compact.

Question 2.11. State and prove the Riesz Lemma.

Exam Answer

Let \(X\) be a normed space and let \(E\subsetneq X\) be a closed vector subspace. For every \(\varepsilon\in(0,1)\) there exists \(x_\varepsilon\in X\) such that \[ \|x_\varepsilon\|=1, \qquad \operatorname{dist}(x_\varepsilon,E)\ge 1-\varepsilon. \]

Proof. Choose \(y\in X\setminus E\). Since \(E\) is closed, \[ d:=\operatorname{dist}(y,E)>0. \] By definition of infimum, choose \(\eta\in E\) such that \[ d\le \|y-\eta\|\le \frac{d}{1-\varepsilon}. \] Set \[ x_\varepsilon:=\frac{y-\eta}{\|y-\eta\|}. \] Then \(\|x_\varepsilon\|=1\). For any \(\xi\in E\), \[ \|x_\varepsilon-\xi\| = \frac{1}{\|y-\eta\|} \left\|y-\big(\eta+\|y-\eta\|\xi\big)\right\|. \] Since \(\eta+\|y-\eta\|\xi\in E\), the last norm is at least \(d\). Hence \[ \|x_\varepsilon-\xi\|\ge \frac d{\|y-\eta\|}\ge 1-\varepsilon. \] Taking the infimum over \(\xi\in E\) gives \[ \operatorname{dist}(x_\varepsilon,E)\ge1-\varepsilon. \]

Question 2.15. Write the definitions ofLp and Lp. Show that Lp is a vector space (and its preliminary lemma).

Exam Answer

Let \((X,\mathcal A,\mu)\) be a measure space and \(1\le p<\infty\). Define \[ \mathcal L^p(X,\mathcal A,\mu) := \left\{f\in\mathcal M(X,\mathcal A):\int_X |f|^p\,d\mu<\infty\right\}. \] Then \[ L^p(X,\mathcal A,\mu):=\mathcal L^p(X,\mathcal A,\mu)/\sim, \qquad f\sim g \iff f=g\ \text{a.e.} \]

Preliminary lemma: for \(a,b\ge0\) and \(p\ge1\), \[ (a+b)^p\le 2^{p-1}(a^p+b^p). \] Indeed, by convexity of \(t\mapsto t^p\), \[ \left(\frac{a+b}{2}\right)^p\le \frac{a^p+b^p}{2}. \]

If \(f,g\in\mathcal L^p\) and \(\lambda\in\mathbb R\), then \(f+\lambda g\) is measurable and \[ |f+\lambda g|^p \le (|f|+|\lambda||g|)^p \le 2^{p-1}\big(|f|^p+|\lambda|^p|g|^p\big). \] The right-hand side is integrable, so \(f+\lambda g\in\mathcal L^p\). Hence \(\mathcal L^p\) is a vector space. The quotient \(L^p\) inherits the vector space structure.

Question 2.19. Show thatLp is a normed space.

Exam Answer

For \(1\le p<\infty\), define \[ \|f\|_p:=\left(\int_X |f|^p\,d\mu\right)^{1/p}, \] and for \(p=\infty\), \[ \|f\|_\infty:=\operatorname*{ess\,sup}_X |f|. \] On \(L^p\), these are norms.

Definiteness: \(\|f\|_p=0\) iff \(f=0\) a.e., which is the zero element in the quotient space. Homogeneity follows from \[ \|\alpha f\|_p=|\alpha|\|f\|_p. \] The triangle inequality is exactly Minkowski: \[ \|f+g\|_p\le\|f\|_p+\|g\|_p. \] Thus \(L^p\) is a normed vector space.

Question 2.20. Show the inclusion ofLp spaces. Which hypothesis is essential? Justify the answer.

Exam Answer

If \(\mu(X)<\infty\) and \(1\le p\le q\le\infty\), then \[ L^q(X)\subseteq L^p(X). \]

If \(q=\infty\), then \(|f|\le\|f\|_\infty\) a.e., hence \[ \|f\|_p\le \mu(X)^{1/p}\|f\|_\infty. \] If \(q<\infty\), apply Holder to \(|f|^p\cdot 1\) with exponents \(q/p\) and \(q/(q-p)\): \[ \|f\|_p^p =\int_X |f|^p\,d\mu \le \left(\int_X |f|^q\,d\mu\right)^{p/q} \mu(X)^{1-p/q}. \] Therefore \[ \|f\|_p\le \mu(X)^{1/p-1/q}\|f\|_q. \]

The hypothesis \(\mu(X)<\infty\) is essential. On \((1,\infty)\) with Lebesgue measure, \[ f(x)=\frac1x \] belongs to \(L^2\) but not to \(L^1\).

Question 2.23. Provide examples of spaces that are dense inLp.For whichp is Lp(R) separable?

Exam Answer

For \(1\le p<\infty\), standard dense subspaces of \(L^p(\Omega)\) include \[ C_c(\Omega) \qquad\text{and}\qquad C_c^\infty(\Omega) \] under the usual hypotheses on \(\Omega\subseteq\mathbb R^N\).

In particular, for \(1\le p<\infty\), \[ C_c(\mathbb R),\ C_c^\infty(\mathbb R) \quad\text{are dense in }L^p(\mathbb R). \]

The space \(L^p(\mathbb R)\) is separable for \[ 1\le p<\infty. \] The space \(L^\infty(\mathbb R)\) is not separable.

Question 2.30. State Baire’s Theorem in its two versions.

Exam Answer

Baire category theorem (version 1: intersection form).
Let \((X,d)\) be a complete metric space. If \(\{A_n\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\) are open and dense in \(X\), then \[ \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty A_n \] is dense in \(X\).

Baire category theorem (version 2: union form).
Let \((X,d)\) be a complete metric space. If \[ X=\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty C_n \] with each \(C_n\) closed, then at least one \(C_{n_0}\) has nonempty interior: \[ \operatorname{Int}(C_{n_0})\neq\varnothing. \] Equivalently, a complete metric space is not a countable union of nowhere dense sets.

Question 2.31. Let F⊂L (X,Y ). For F write the definition of pointwise and uniform boundedness. State and prove the UBP (or BS theorem).

Exam Answer

Definitions.
Let \(X,Y\) be Banach spaces and \(\mathcal{F}\subset \mathcal{L}(X,Y)\).

  • Pointwise boundedness: \[ \forall x\in X,\quad \sup_{T\in\mathcal{F}}\|Tx\|_Y<\infty. \]
  • Uniform boundedness: \[ \sup_{T\in\mathcal{F}}\|T\|_{\mathcal{L}(X,Y)}<\infty. \]

Uniform Boundedness Principle (Banach-Steinhaus).
If \(X\) is Banach and \(\mathcal{F}\subset \mathcal{L}(X,Y)\) is pointwise bounded, then \(\mathcal{F}\) is uniformly bounded.

Proof (via Baire theorem).
For each \(n\in\mathbb{N}\) define \[ C_n:=\{x\in X:\ \|Tx\|_Y\le n\ \forall T\in\mathcal{F}\}. \] Each \(C_n\) is closed (intersection of closed sets \(\{x:\|Tx\|\le n\}\)).
Pointwise boundedness implies \[ X=\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty C_n. \] Since \(X\) is complete, Baire implies: there exists \(n_0\) such that \(\operatorname{Int}(C_{n_0})\neq\varnothing\).
So there exist \(x_0\in X\), \(\varepsilon>0\) with \[ \overline{B_\varepsilon(x_0)}\subset C_{n_0}. \] Hence, for every \(T\in\mathcal{F}\) and every \(z\) with \(\|z\|\le\varepsilon\): \[ \|T(x_0+z)\|\le n_0,\qquad \|T(x_0)\|\le n_0, \] thus \[ \|Tz\|=\|T(x_0+z)-T(x_0)\| \le \|T(x_0+z)\|+\|T(x_0)\| \le 2n_0. \] For arbitrary \(x\neq 0\), set \(z=\varepsilon x/\|x\|\) (so \(\|z\|=\varepsilon\)). Then \[ \left\|T\!\left(\frac{\varepsilon x}{\|x\|}\right)\right\|\le 2n_0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \|Tx\|\le \frac{2n_0}{\varepsilon}\|x\|. \] Therefore \[ \|T\|_{\mathcal{L}(X,Y)}\le \frac{2n_0}{\varepsilon} \quad\forall T\in\mathcal{F}, \] so \(\mathcal{F}\) is uniformly bounded.

Question 2.33. Write the definition of open mapping.

Exam Answer

Let \(X,Y\) be metric spaces. A map \(T:X\to Y\) is open if for every open set \(A\subseteq X\) the image \(T(A)\) is open in \(Y\).

For linear maps, the Open Mapping Theorem says: if \(X,Y\) are Banach spaces and \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\) is surjective, then \(T\) is open.

Question 2.34. State and prove the IBM (or ICM) theorem. What is the analogous result in the finite dimensional case?

Exam Answer

Let \(X,Y\) be Banach spaces and let \(T\in\mathcal L(X,Y)\) be bijective. Then \[ T^{-1}\in\mathcal L(Y,X). \]

Proof. Since \(T\) is bijective, \(T^{-1}:Y\to X\) is linear. To prove continuity, let \(A\subseteq X\) be open. Then \[ (T^{-1})^{-1}(A)=T(A). \] Since \(T\) is continuous, linear, and surjective between Banach spaces, the Open Mapping Theorem implies that \(T(A)\) is open in \(Y\). Hence \(T^{-1}\) is continuous, i.e. bounded.

In finite dimension, the analogous result is automatic: every linear map between finite-dimensional normed spaces is continuous. Thus a bijective linear map has a continuous inverse without requiring Banach-space arguments.

Question 2.35. By the IBM theorem we can infer an important property about equivalent norms on Banach spaces. What is that? Justify your answer.

Exam Answer

Let \(\|\cdot\|_1\) and \(\|\cdot\|_2\) be two norms on \(X\) such that both \((X,\|\cdot\|_1)\) and \((X,\|\cdot\|_2)\) are Banach spaces. If there exists \(M>0\) with \[ \|x\|_2\le M\|x\|_1\qquad\forall x\in X, \] then the norms are equivalent: there exists \(m>0\) such that \[ m\|x\|_1\le \|x\|_2\le M\|x\|_1. \]

Proof. Consider the identity map \[ I:(X,\|\cdot\|_1)\to(X,\|\cdot\|_2), \qquad I(x)=x. \] The assumption says that \(I\) is bounded. It is linear and bijective. By the inverse bounded mapping theorem, \(I^{-1}\) is bounded, so for some \(C>0\), \[ \|x\|_1\le C\|x\|_2. \] Taking \(m=1/C\) gives the reverse inequality.

Question 2.36. Write the definitions of: closed operator; graph of an operator. Show that an operator is linear and closed iff its graph is closed.

Exam Answer

Let \(X,Y\) be normed spaces and let \(T:X\to Y\) be linear. The graph of \(T\) is \[ \operatorname{graph}(T):=\{(x,Tx):x\in X\}\subset X\times Y. \] The product space may be endowed with, for example, \[ \|(x,y)\|_{X\times Y}:=\|x\|_X+\|y\|_Y. \]

The operator \(T\) is closed if whenever \[ x_n\to x \quad\text{in }X, \qquad Tx_n\to y \quad\text{in }Y, \] then \[ y=Tx. \]

Claim: \[ T\text{ is closed} \iff \operatorname{graph}(T)\text{ is closed in }X\times Y. \]

Proof. If \(T\) is closed and \((x_n,Tx_n)\to(x,y)\) in \(X\times Y\), then \(x_n\to x\) and \(Tx_n\to y\). By closedness, \(y=Tx\), hence \((x,y)\in\operatorname{graph}(T)\).

Conversely, assume \(\operatorname{graph}(T)\) is closed. If \(x_n\to x\) and \(Tx_n\to y\), then \[ (x_n,Tx_n)\to(x,y). \] Since the graph is closed, \((x,y)\in\operatorname{graph}(T)\), so \(y=Tx\). Hence \(T\) is closed.

Question 2.37. State and prove the closed graph theorem.

Exam Answer

Let \(X,Y\) be Banach spaces. If \(T:X\to Y\) is linear and closed, then \(T\) is bounded, hence continuous: \[ T\in\mathcal L(X,Y). \]

Proof. Define the graph norm on \(X\) by \[ \|x\|_G:=\|x\|_X+\|Tx\|_Y. \] This is a norm. Since \(T\) is closed, \(\operatorname{graph}(T)\) is a closed subspace of the Banach space \(X\times Y\), hence is Banach. The map \[ x\mapsto(x,Tx) \] is an isometry from \((X,\|\cdot\|_G)\) onto \(\operatorname{graph}(T)\), so \((X,\|\cdot\|_G)\) is Banach.

Also, \[ \|x\|_X\le\|x\|_G\qquad\forall x\in X. \] Thus the identity map \[ I:(X,\|\cdot\|_G)\to(X,\|\cdot\|_X) \] is bounded and bijective between Banach spaces. By the inverse bounded mapping theorem, \(I^{-1}\) is bounded. Hence there exists \(C>0\) such that \[ \|x\|_G\le C\|x\|_X. \] Therefore \[ \|Tx\|_Y\le \|x\|_G\le C\|x\|_X, \] so \(T\) is bounded.

Question 2.42. State the Hahn-Banach theorem in the continuous extension form.

Exam Answer

Let \(X\) be a normed space and let \(Y\subset X\) be a vector subspace. If \(f\in Y^*\), then there exists \(F\in X^*\) such that \[ F|_Y=f \qquad\text{and}\qquad \|F\|_{X^*}=\|f\|_{Y^*}. \] Thus every continuous linear functional on a subspace extends to the whole space without increasing its norm.

Question 2.44. State the Hahn-Banach theorem in the separation form (first and second version).

Exam Answer

Let \(X\) be a normed space. A closed hyperplane \[ H=\{x\in X:L(x)=\alpha\}, \qquad L\in X^*,\ L\ne0, \] separates \(A,B\subset X\) if \[ L(a)\le\alpha\le L(b) \qquad\forall a\in A,\ b\in B. \] It strictly separates them if there exists \(\varepsilon>0\) such that \[ L(a)\le\alpha-\varepsilon<\alpha+\varepsilon\le L(b). \]

First separation theorem: if \(A,B\subset X\) are nonempty, disjoint, convex, and \(A\) is open, then there exists a closed hyperplane separating \(A\) and \(B\).

Second separation theorem: if \(A,B\subset X\) are nonempty, disjoint, convex, \(A\) is closed, and \(B\) is compact, then there exists a closed hyperplane strictly separating \(A\) and \(B\).

Question 2.45. State and prove the three corollaries of the Hanh-Banach Theorem.

Exam Answer

  1. If \(x_0\in X\), \(x_0\ne0\), then there exists \(L\in X^*\) such that \[ \|L\|=1, \qquad L(x_0)=\|x_0\|. \] Proof. Define \(L_0\) on \(\operatorname{span}\{x_0\}\) by \[ L_0(\lambda x_0)=\lambda\|x_0\|. \] Then \(\|L_0\|=1\). Extend \(L_0\) by Hahn-Banach.

  2. If \(L(y)=L(z)\) for every \(L\in X^*\), then \(y=z\).

Proof. If \(y\ne z\), set \(x_0=y-z\ne0\). By the first corollary, choose \(L\in X^*\) with \(L(x_0)\ne0\), contradiction.

  1. If \(Y\subset X\) is a subspace and \(x_0\notin\overline Y\), then there exists \(L\in X^*\) such that \[ L|_Y=0, \qquad L(x_0)\ne0. \] Proof. Define \(L_0\) on \(Y+\operatorname{span}\{x_0\}\) by \[ L_0(y+\lambda x_0)=\lambda. \] This is well-defined because \(x_0\notin\overline Y\), hence \(x_0\notin Y\). It is continuous since \(\operatorname{dist}(x_0,Y)>0\). Extend \(L_0\) by Hahn-Banach.

Question 2.49. Write the definition of bidual space. Introduce the canonical (or evaluation) map. Show that it is linear and that it preserves the norm.

Exam Answer

Bidual space and canonical map.
Let \(X\) be a normed space and \(X^*=\mathcal{L}(X,\mathbb{R})\). The bidual is \[ X^{**}:=(X^*)^*=\mathcal{L}(X^*,\mathbb{R}). \] For each \(x\in X\), define \(\Lambda_x\in X^{**}\) by \[ \Lambda_x(L):=L(x)\qquad \forall L\in X^*. \] The canonical (evaluation) map is \[ \tau:X\to X^{**},\qquad \tau(x):=\Lambda_x. \]

Linearity of \(\tau\).
For \(x,y\in X\), \(\alpha,\beta\in\mathbb{R}\), and any \(L\in X^*\): \[ \tau(\alpha x+\beta y)(L) =L(\alpha x+\beta y) =\alpha L(x)+\beta L(y) =(\alpha\tau(x)+\beta\tau(y))(L). \] Hence \(\tau(\alpha x+\beta y)=\alpha\tau(x)+\beta\tau(y)\).

Norm preservation (isometry).
For \(x\in X\): \[ \|\tau(x)\|_{X^{**}} =\sup_{\|L\|_{X^*}\le 1}|\tau(x)(L)| =\sup_{\|L\|\le 1}|L(x)| \le \|x\|_X. \] For \(x\neq 0\), by Hahn-Banach (norm-attaining corollary), there exists \(L_x\in X^*\) with \[ \|L_x\|_{X^*}=1,\qquad L_x(x)=\|x\|_X. \] Therefore \[ \|\tau(x)\|_{X^{**}} =\sup_{\|L\|\le 1}|L(x)| \ge |L_x(x)|=\|x\|_X. \] Combining both inequalities: \[ \|\tau(x)\|_{X^{**}}=\|x\|_X\qquad \forall x\in X. \] So \(\tau\) is an isometric embedding.

Reflexivity note.
\(X\) is reflexive iff \(\tau\) is surjective.

Question 2.50. Write the definition of reflexive space. LetX be a reflexive space andφ∈ X∗∗. What aboutφ(L) for anyL∈X∗?

Exam Answer

Let \(X\) be a normed space. The canonical map is \[ \tau:X\to X^{**}, \qquad \tau(x)(L)=L(x)\quad \forall L\in X^*. \] The space \(X\) is reflexive if \(\tau\) is surjective, i.e. \[ \tau(X)=X^{**}. \]

If \(X\) is reflexive and \(\varphi\in X^{**}\), then there exists \(x\in X\) such that \[ \varphi=\tau(x). \] Therefore, for every \(L\in X^*\), \[ \varphi(L)=\tau(x)(L)=L(x). \] So every element of \(X^{**}\) is evaluation at some point of \(X\).

Question 2.51. State the Milman-Pettis theorem. Show thatLp is reflexive for anyp∈ (1, +∞). What aboutL1 and L∞?

Exam Answer

Milman-Pettis theorem: every uniformly convex Banach space is reflexive.

For \(1<p<\infty\), Clarkson inequalities imply that \(L^p\) is uniformly convex. Since \(L^p\) is also complete, Milman-Pettis gives \[ L^p \text{ is reflexive for }1<p<\infty. \]

In general, \[ L^1 \text{ and } L^\infty \] are not reflexive. One reason is that \((L^\infty)^*\) is strictly larger than \(L^1\) in the usual nontrivial measure spaces, so the canonical embedding of \(L^1\) into its bidual cannot be onto. Also \(L^\infty\) fails reflexivity in standard infinite-dimensional settings.

Question 2.68. State and prove the projection theorem and its corollary in Hilbert spaces. State and give an idea of the proof of the projection theorem inLp.

Exam Answer

Projection theorem in Hilbert spaces.
Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space and \(K\subset H\) nonempty, closed, convex.
For every \(f\in H\), there exists a unique \(u\in K\) such that \[ \|f-u\|=\inf_{v\in K}\|f-v\|. \] Moreover, \(u\) is characterized by \[ \langle f-u,\ v-u\rangle \le 0 \qquad \forall v\in K. \]

Proof (existence and uniqueness sketch).
Let \[ d:=\inf_{v\in K}\|f-v\|, \] and choose a minimizing sequence \(v_n\in K\) with \(\|f-v_n\|\to d\).
By the parallelogram law: \[ \|v_n-v_m\|^2 = 2\big(\|f-v_n\|^2+\|f-v_m\|^2\big) - 4\left\|f-\frac{v_n+v_m}{2}\right\|^2. \] Since \(K\) is convex, \(\frac{v_n+v_m}{2}\in K\), hence \[ \left\|f-\frac{v_n+v_m}{2}\right\|\ge d. \] Therefore the right-hand side tends to \(0\) as \(n,m\to\infty\), so \(v_n\) is Cauchy.
Completeness of \(H\) and closedness of \(K\) give \(v_n\to u\in K\), and \(\|f-u\|=d\).

Uniqueness follows similarly from the parallelogram identity: if \(u,\tilde u\) are both minimizers, then midpoint convexity implies \(\|u-\tilde u\|=0\).

Variational characterization.
- If \(u\) is minimizer, for \(v\in K\) and \(t\in(0,1)\) set \(w_t=u+t(v-u)\in K\).
From \(\|f-u\|^2\le \|f-w_t\|^2\), differentiate at \(t=0^+\) to get \[ \langle f-u,\ v-u\rangle\le 0. \] - Conversely, if the inequality holds for all \(v\in K\), expand \[ \|f-v\|^2=\|f-u\|^2+\|v-u\|^2-2\langle f-u,\ v-u\rangle \ge \|f-u\|^2, \] hence \(u\) is a minimizer.

Corollary (closed subspace).
If \(M\subset H\) is a closed subspace, then for each \(f\in H\) there exists unique \(u=P_Mf\in M\) such that \[ \langle f-u,\ v\rangle=0\qquad \forall v\in M. \] Equivalently, \(f-u\in M^\perp\) and \(f=u+(f-u)\) with \(u\in M\), \(f-u\in M^\perp\).

\(L^p\) version (\(1<p<\infty\)). If \(K\subset L^p\) is nonempty, closed, convex, then each \(f\in L^p\) has a unique best approximation in \(K\).
The proof replaces the Hilbert parallelogram argument with uniform convexity (e.g. Clarkson inequalities), which yields Cauchy property of minimizing sequences and uniqueness.
This fails in general for \(p=1\) or \(p=\infty\) (lack of uniform convexity).

Question 2.69. State and prove the Riesz theorem in Hilbert spaces. State and give and idea of the proof of the Riesz theorem inLp.

Exam Answer

Riesz representation theorem (Hilbert spaces). Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space. For every \(\varphi\in H^*\) there exists a unique \(f\in H\) such that \[ \varphi(u)=\langle f,u\rangle \qquad \forall u\in H. \] Moreover, \[ \|\varphi\|_{H^*}=\|f\|_H. \]

Proof (Hilbert case).
- If \(\varphi=0\), take \(f=0\).
- Assume \(\varphi\neq 0\). Let \(M:=\ker\varphi\), a closed subspace of \(H\). Since \(M\neq H\), choose \(g\in M^\perp\setminus\{0\}\) and normalize \(\|g\|=1\). - For any \(u\in H\), set \[ v:=u-\lambda g,\qquad \lambda:=\frac{\varphi(u)}{\varphi(g)}. \] Then \(\varphi(v)=0\), so \(v\in M\). As \(g\in M^\perp\), \(\langle g,v\rangle=0\), hence \[ \langle g,u\rangle=\lambda. \] Therefore \[ \varphi(u)=\varphi(g)\langle g,u\rangle =\langle \varphi(g)g,\ u\rangle. \] So take \(f:=\varphi(g)g\). - Uniqueness: if \(\langle f_1,u\rangle=\langle f_2,u\rangle\) for all \(u\), then \[ \langle f_1-f_2,u\rangle=0\ \forall u. \] Choose \(u=f_1-f_2\) to get \(f_1=f_2\). - Isometry: \[ |\varphi(u)|=|\langle f,u\rangle|\le \|f\|\,\|u\| \Rightarrow \|\varphi\|\le \|f\|. \] If \(f\neq 0\), test with \(u=f/\|f\|\): \[ \|\varphi\|\ge |\varphi(f/\|f\|)|=\|f\|. \] Hence \(\|\varphi\|=\|f\|\).

\(L^p\) Riesz representation (\(1<p<\infty\)). Let \(q=p'\), \(\frac1p+\frac1q=1\). For every \(\Lambda\in (L^p)^*\) there exists unique \(g\in L^q\) such that \[ \Lambda(f)=\int_X f g\,d\mu\qquad \forall f\in L^p, \] and \[ \|\Lambda\|_{(L^p)^*}=\|g\|_{L^q}. \] Proof idea. Use Holder for boundedness of \(f\mapsto\int fg\) and the geometry of \(L^p\) to identify every \(\Lambda\in(L^p)^*\) with a unique \(g\in L^q\).

Question 2.70. Write the theorem about the abstract Fourier series in Hilbert spaces. Write Parseval’s identity.

Exam Answer

Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space and let \(\{e_k\}_{k\ge1}\) be an orthonormal basis of \(H\). Then, for every \(u\in H\), \[ u=\sum_{k=1}^\infty \langle u,e_k\rangle e_k \] with convergence in \(H\). This is the abstract Fourier series of \(u\), and the numbers \(\langle u,e_k\rangle\) are its Fourier coefficients.

Parseval identity: \[ \|u\|^2=\sum_{k=1}^\infty |\langle u,e_k\rangle|^2. \]

Question 2.71. Write the definition of orthonormal basis of a Hilbert space. Exhibit some examples.

Exam Answer

Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space. A sequence \(\{e_n\}\subset H\) is an orthonormal basis if: \[ \langle e_n,e_m\rangle=0\quad(n\ne m), \qquad \|e_n\|=1\quad\forall n, \] and \[ \overline{\operatorname{span}\{e_n:n\in\mathbb N\}}=H. \]

Examples: 1. In \(\ell^2\), the canonical vectors \(e_n=(0,\dots,0,1,0,\dots)\) form an orthonormal basis. 2. In \(L^2(0,2\pi)\), the trigonometric system gives a standard orthonormal basis after normalization.

Question 2.72. What is it possible to say about convergence of an o.n.b.?

Exam Answer

If \(\{e_n\}\) is an orthonormal basis of an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space \(H\), then \[ e_n\rightharpoonup0 \quad\text{but}\quad e_n\not\to0 \text{ strongly}. \]

Proof. For any \(u\in H\), Parseval gives \[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty |\langle u,e_n\rangle|^2\le \|u\|^2<\infty. \] Hence \(\langle u,e_n\rangle\to0\). By Riesz representation, every \(L\in H^*\) has the form \(L(v)=\langle v,u\rangle\) for some \(u\in H\), so \[ L(e_n)\to0\qquad\forall L\in H^*. \] Thus \(e_n\rightharpoonup0\).

Strong convergence to \(0\) fails because \[ \|e_n\|=1\qquad\forall n. \]

Question 2.73. Write the definition of symmetric operator on Hilbert spaces. How is it possible to compute its norm?

Exam Answer

Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space. An operator \(T\in\mathcal L(H)\) is symmetric if \[ \langle Tx,y\rangle=\langle x,Ty\rangle \qquad\forall x,y\in H. \]

For symmetric bounded operators, \[ \|T\|_{\mathcal L(H)} = \sup_{\|x\|=1}|\langle Tx,x\rangle|. \] Thus the operator norm can be computed from the quadratic form associated with \(T\).

Question 2.74. State the Fredholm alternative. Discuss the solvability of equations on Hilbert spaces.

Exam Answer

Let \(H\) be a Hilbert space and let \(T\in\mathcal K(H)\) be compact and symmetric. Consider \[ u-Tu=f. \] The Fredholm alternative states:

  1. \(\ker(I-T)\) is finite-dimensional.
  2. \(\operatorname{Im}(I-T)\) is closed and \[ \operatorname{Im}(I-T)=\ker(I-T)^\perp. \]
  3. \(I-T\) is injective iff it is surjective.

Therefore:

If \(\ker(I-T)=\{0\}\), then for every \(f\in H\) there exists a unique solution \(u\in H\).

If \(\ker(I-T)\ne\{0\}\), then the equation is solvable iff \[ f\in\ker(I-T)^\perp. \] In that case solutions are not unique: if \(u\) is one solution, then \(u+v\) is also a solution for every \(v\in\ker(I-T)\).

Question 2.75. Write the definitions ofρ(T ),σ (T ),EV (T ),σ p(T ). What is the structure of the spectrum of a compact operator?

Exam Answer

Let \(T\in\mathcal{L}(E)\) on a Banach space \(E\).

Definitions. - Resolvent set: \[ \rho(T):=\{\lambda\in\mathbb{K}:\ T-\lambda I \text{ is bijective and }(T-\lambda I)^{-1}\in\mathcal{L}(E)\}. \] - Spectrum: \[ \sigma(T):=\mathbb{K}\setminus \rho(T). \] - Point spectrum / eigenvalues: \[ \sigma_p(T)=EV(T):=\{\lambda\in\mathbb{K}:\ker(T-\lambda I)\neq\{0\}\}. \]

Spectrum of a compact operator.
If \(E\) is infinite-dimensional and \(T\in\mathcal{K}(E)\), then: 1. \(0\in\sigma(T)\); 2. every nonzero spectral value is an eigenvalue: \[ \sigma(T)\setminus\{0\}=\sigma_p(T)\setminus\{0\}; \] 3. \(\sigma(T)\setminus\{0\}\) is at most countable, and \(0\) is its only possible accumulation point.

Equivalently, either \(\sigma(T)=\{0\}\), or \(\sigma(T)\setminus\{0\}\) is finite, or it is a sequence converging to \(0\).

Question 2.76. State the spectral theorem.

Exam Answer

Let \(H\) be a separable Hilbert space and let \[ T\in\mathcal K(H) \] be compact and symmetric. Then there exists an orthonormal basis of \(H\) made of eigenvectors of \(T\).

Equivalently, \(H\) admits an orthonormal basis \(\{e_n\}\) such that \[ Te_n=\lambda_n e_n \] for suitable real eigenvalues \(\lambda_n\). The nonzero eigenvalues have finite multiplicity and can accumulate only at \(0\).

Question 2.16. Write the definition of conjugate numbers. Show the Young inequality.

Exam Answer

Numbers \(p,q\in[1,\infty]\) are conjugate if \[ \frac1p+\frac1q=1, \] with the conventions \(1/\infty=0\), so \(1\) and \(\infty\) are conjugate.

Young inequality: if \(p,q\in(1,\infty)\) are conjugate and \(a,b\ge0\), then \[ ab\le \frac{a^p}{p}+\frac{b^q}{q}. \]

Proof. If \(a=0\) or \(b=0\), the result is trivial. For \(a,b>0\), \[ ab=\exp\left(\frac1p\log a^p+\frac1q\log b^q\right). \] Since \(\exp\) is convex, \[ \exp\left(\frac1p\log a^p+\frac1q\log b^q\right) \le \frac1p e^{\log a^p}+\frac1q e^{\log b^q} =\frac{a^p}{p}+\frac{b^q}{q}. \]

Question 2.18. Write and show the Minkowski inequality.

Exam Answer

For \(1\le p\le\infty\) and \(f,g\in L^p\), \[ \|f+g\|_p\le \|f\|_p+\|g\|_p. \]

For \(p=1\) this follows from \(|f+g|\le |f|+|g|\). For \(p=\infty\), \[ \|f+g\|_\infty\le \|f\|_\infty+\|g\|_\infty. \]

Let \(1<p<\infty\) and let \(q\) be conjugate to \(p\). If \(\|f+g\|_p=0\), there is nothing to prove. Otherwise, \[ \|f+g\|_p^p =\int |f+g|^p \le \int (|f|+|g|)|f+g|^{p-1}. \] By Holder, \[ \int |f||f+g|^{p-1} \le \|f\|_p\left\||f+g|^{p-1}\right\|_q, \] and similarly for \(g\). Since \((p-1)q=p\), \[ \left\||f+g|^{p-1}\right\|_q=\|f+g\|_p^{p-1}. \] Thus \[ \|f+g\|_p^p \le (\|f\|_p+\|g\|_p)\|f+g\|_p^{p-1}, \] and division by \(\|f+g\|_p^{p-1}\) gives the result.

Question 2.21. State and prove the interpolation inequality.

Exam Answer

Let \(1\le p\le r\le q\le\infty\) and let \(f\in L^p\cap L^q\). If \(\alpha\in[0,1]\) satisfies \[ \frac1r=\frac{\alpha}{p}+\frac{1-\alpha}{q}, \] then \[ \|f\|_r\le \|f\|_p^\alpha\|f\|_q^{1-\alpha}. \]

Assume first \(q<\infty\) and \(p<r<q\). Then \[ \|f\|_r^r=\int |f|^{\alpha r}|f|^{(1-\alpha)r}. \] Apply Holder with conjugate exponents \[ P=\frac{p}{\alpha r}, \qquad Q=\frac{q}{(1-\alpha)r}, \] equivalently \(1/P+1/Q=1\), which follows from the relation defining \(\alpha\). Then \[ \|f\|_r^r \le \left(\int |f|^p\right)^{\alpha r/p} \left(\int |f|^q\right)^{(1-\alpha)r/q} = \|f\|_p^{\alpha r}\|f\|_q^{(1-\alpha)r}. \] Taking the \(r\)-th root gives the result. The endpoint cases follow directly; for \(q=\infty\), use \(|f|^{(1-\alpha)r}\le\|f\|_\infty^{(1-\alpha)r}\) a.e.

Question 2.59. Write the definition of weak* convergence.

Exam Answer

Definition (weak* convergence in \(X^*\)).
Let \(X\) be a normed space and \(\{L_n\}\subset X^*\). We say \[ L_n \stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup} L \] if and only if \[ L_n(x)\to L(x)\qquad \forall x\in X. \]

Useful implication: In \(X^*\): norm convergence \(\Rightarrow\) weak convergence \(\Rightarrow\) weak* convergence.

Question 2.60. Which is the relation between weak and weak∗ convergence? Justify your answer.

Exam Answer

Theorem. \[ L_n \rightharpoonup L \text{ in }X^* \ \Longrightarrow\ L_n \stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup} L \text{ in }X^*. \]

Proof.
If \(L_n\rightharpoonup L\), then \[ \Lambda(L_n)\to \Lambda(L)\qquad \forall \Lambda\in X^{**}. \] For each \(x\in X\), consider the canonical embedding \(\tau(x)\in X^{**}\): \[ \tau(x)(L)=L(x). \] Since \(\tau(x)\in X^{**}\), we get \[ \tau(x)(L_n)\to \tau(x)(L), \] i.e. \[ L_n(x)\to L(x)\qquad \forall x\in X. \] Hence \(L_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}L\).

Converse.
In general, weak* convergence does not imply weak convergence (unless \(X\) is reflexive).
If \(X\) is reflexive, \(X^{**}\cong X\), so weak and weak* convergence in \(X^*\) coincide.

Question 2.61. Write the properties of weak∗ convergence.

Exam Answer

Let \(L_n \stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup} L\) in \(X^*\). Main properties:

  1. Uniqueness of limit.
    If \(L_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}L\) and \(L_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}M\), then \(L=M\). Proof: for every \(x\in X\), \(L_n(x)\to L(x)\) and \(L_n(x)\to M(x)\), so \(L(x)=M(x)\) for all \(x\).

  2. Boundedness. \[ \sup_n \|L_n\|_{X^*}<\infty. \] Proof: for each fixed \(x\), \(\{L_n(x)\}\) converges, hence is bounded.
    Apply Uniform Boundedness Principle to \(\{L_n\}\subset \mathcal{L}(X,\mathbb{R})\).

  3. Lower semicontinuity. \[ \|L\|_{X^*}\le \liminf_{n\to\infty}\|L_n\|_{X^*}. \] Proof: for any \(\|x\|\le 1\), \(L_n(x)\to L(x)\), so \[ |L(x)|=\lim_n |L_n(x)|\le \liminf_n \|L_n\|. \] Take supremum over \(\|x\|\le 1\).

  4. Composition with strong convergence. If \(x_n\to x\) strongly in \(X\) and \(L_n\stackrel{*}{\rightharpoonup}L\) in \(X^*\), then \[ L_n(x_n)\to L(x). \] Proof: \[ L_n(x_n)-L(x)=L_n(x_n-x)+(L_n(x)-L(x)). \] Second term \(\to 0\) by weak* convergence at fixed \(x\).
    First term \(\to 0\) because \(\sup_n\|L_n\|<\infty\) and \(\|x_n-x\|\to 0\).
